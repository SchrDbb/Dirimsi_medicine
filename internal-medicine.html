Case 1: Myocardial Infarction, Acute

Case Description

A 56-year-old man presents to the ER with severe retrosternal chest discomfort, awakened from sleep 3 hours earlier. He has a history of hypercholesterolemia and a 40-pack-year smoking history. Examination shows discomfort, diaphoresis, heart rate of 116 bpm, blood pressure of 166/102 mm Hg, respiratory rate of 22 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 96%. Chest is clear, with a regular rhythm, S4 gallop, and no murmurs. Chest radiograph is normal, and ECG is provided.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the next step in therapy?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Acute ST-segment elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI).
Next step in therapy: Administer aspirin and a beta-blocker, and assess for rapid reperfusion (thrombolytics or percutaneous coronary intervention).

Analysis

Objectives

  • Know the diagnostic criteria for acute MI.
  • Understand which patients should receive thrombolytics or PCI.
  • Be familiar with MI complications and treatments.
  • Understand post-MI risk stratification and secondary prevention.

Considerations

Suspicion of acute MI based on clinical and ECG findings, deciding on thrombolytics or PCI, and excluding other diagnoses like pericarditis or aortic dissection.

Approach to Suspected MI

Definitions

  • Acute Coronary Syndrome: Spectrum from unstable angina to acute MI.
  • Acute Myocardial Infarction: Death of myocardial tissue due to inadequate blood flow.
  • NSTEMI: MI without ST-segment elevation, with other ECG changes or elevated biomarkers.
  • PCI: Percutaneous coronary intervention (angioplasty/stenting).
  • STEMI: MI with ST-segment elevation >0.1 mV in two or more contiguous leads.
  • Thrombolytics: Drugs to lyse fibrin thrombi and restore coronary artery patency.

Clinical Approach

Describes pathophysiology, diagnostic criteria (history, physical findings, ECG, cardiac biomarkers), and treatment (aspirin, heparin, beta-blockers, nitrates, morphine, oxygen, reperfusion). Includes complications like arrhythmias, pump failure, and mechanical issues.

Comprehension Questions

  1. A 36-year-old woman with chest pain after meals...
  2. A 56-year-old man with chest pain and sinus bradycardia...
  3. A 59-year-old diabetic woman post-MI with elevated troponin...
  4. A 59-year-old man with substernal chest pain and normal ECG...

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. B (Proton-pump inhibitor for reflux esophagitis).
2. B (Inferior wall MI).
3. D (Serial ECGs and CK-MB).
4. C (Aspirin).

Clinical Pearls

  • Acute coronary syndromes result from thrombus at a ruptured atherosclerotic plaque.
  • MI diagnosis requires at least two of: typical symptoms, ECG findings, cardiac enzymes.
  • Early reperfusion reduces mortality in STEMI.
  • Secondary prevention includes smoking cessation, aspirin, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins.

References

Antman EM, et al. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 18th ed. 2012.

Case 2: Congestive Heart Failure due to Critical Aortic Stenosis

Case Description

A 72-year-old man presents with worsening exertional dyspnea, angina-like chest pressure, near-syncope, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, and pedal edema. Examination shows distended neck veins, bibasilar crackles, regular rhythm with S4, late-peaking systolic murmur, and diminished carotid upstrokes.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What test would confirm the diagnosis?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Congestive heart failure (CHF), possibly due to aortic stenosis.
Diagnostic test: Echocardiogram to assess aortic valve area and left ventricular function.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Know causes of chronic heart failure.
  • Recognize systolic vs. diastolic dysfunction.
  • Understand treatment of acute and chronic heart failure.
  • Evaluate aortic stenosis and indications for valve replacement.

Considerations

Elderly patient with progressive symptoms from angina to heart failure, suggesting severe aortic stenosis requiring urgent evaluation.

Approach to Congestive Heart Failure

Definitions

  • Acute Heart Failure: Rapid cardiac decompensation with pulmonary edema.
  • Chronic Heart Failure: Long-term cardiac dysfunction.
  • Diastolic Dysfunction: Impaired relaxation with preserved ejection fraction.
  • Systolic Dysfunction: Low cardiac output with low ejection fraction.
  • Cardiac Remodeling: Changes due to increased cardiac load.

Clinical Approach

Details CHF as a syndrome from underlying heart disease, NYHA classification, causes, and treatments (diuretics, ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, CRT, ICD). Aortic stenosis specifics include symptoms, physical findings, and valve replacement indications.

Comprehension Questions

  1. A 55-year-old man with severe CHF...
  2. Most likely cause of CHF in the U.S....
  3. A 75-year-old man with chest pain and systolic murmur...
  4. A 55-year-old man with CHF and 47% ejection fraction...

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. A (ACE inhibitors).
2. B (Atherosclerosis).
3. C (Valve replacement).
4. A (Diastolic dysfunction).

Clinical Pearls

  • CHF is caused by underlying heart disease, commonly ischemic cardiomyopathy or hypertension.
  • Systolic and diastolic dysfunction differ in ejection fraction.
  • ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and aldosterone antagonists reduce mortality.
  • Aortic stenosis requires valve replacement for severe cases.

References

Carabello BA, et al. N Engl J Med. 2002; Jessup M, et al. N Engl J Med. 2003.

Case 3: Atrial Fibrillation, Mitral Stenosis

Case Description

A 26-year-old pregnant woman with a history of rheumatic fever presents with acute palpitations and atrial fibrillation (AF) with rapid ventricular response. She has a diastolic rumble suggestive of mitral stenosis, causing pulmonary edema.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the next step?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Atrial fibrillation caused by mitral stenosis.
Next step: Cardiac rate control with intravenous beta-blockers.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Know causes of atrial fibrillation.
  • Manage acute AF with rapid ventricular response.
  • Understand anticoagulation in chronic AF.
  • Know cardiac lesions of rheumatic heart disease.
  • Understand Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome considerations.

Considerations

AF due to mitral stenosis, exacerbated by pregnancy-related increased blood volume, requires urgent rate control.

Approach to Atrial Fibrillation

Definitions

  • Atrial Fibrillation: Irregular heart rhythm with chaotic atrial signals.
  • DC Cardioversion: Electrical shock to restore normal rhythm.

Clinical Approach

Details AF causes, management (rate control, anticoagulation, cardioversion), and risks (stroke). Discusses mitral stenosis and WPW syndrome specifics.

Comprehension Questions

  1. Murmur in a woman with rheumatic heart disease...
  2. Management for a woman with AF and hypotension...
  3. Which patient can discontinue anticoagulation...
  4. Management for high INR in an asymptomatic patient...
  5. Treatment for AF with WPW...

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. A (Diastolic rumble).
2. B (DC cardioversion).
3. A (Lone AF).
4. D (Hold warfarin).
5. D (Procainamide).

Clinical Pearls

  • Common AF causes include hypertension and atherosclerosis.
  • Acute AF with instability requires DC cardioversion.
  • Chronic AF usually needs anticoagulation, except in lone AF.
  • WPW with AF avoids AV nodal blockers; use procainamide.

Case 4: Peptic Ulcer Disease

Case Description

A 37-year-old man presents with chronic burning epigastric pain, worse on an empty stomach, relieved by food or antacids. He has a positive H. pylori antibody test, no NSAIDs use, and no alarm symptoms.

Questions

  • What is your diagnosis?
  • What is your next step?

Answers

Diagnosis: Peptic ulcer disease (PUD).
Next step: Triple antibiotic therapy for H. pylori and acid suppression.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Differentiate causes of abdominal pain.
  • Recognize features of duodenal and gastric ulcers.
  • Understand H. pylori and NSAID roles in PUD.
  • Interpret H. pylori tests.

Considerations

Symptoms suggest duodenal ulcer; H. pylori requires eradication to prevent recurrence.

Approach to Peptic Ulcer Disease

Definitions

  • Dyspepsia: Upper abdominal pain or discomfort.
  • Functional Dyspepsia: Symptoms without ulcer evidence.
  • H. pylori: Gram-negative bacillus causing gastric inflammation.
  • PUD: Gastric or duodenal mucosal ulceration.

Clinical Approach

Details dyspepsia causes, ulcer symptoms, H. pylori testing/treatment, NSAID effects, and complications like bleeding or perforation.

Comprehension Questions

  1. Cause of right-upper quadrant pain post-meal...
  2. Accuracy of H. pylori statements...
  3. Management for hematemesis and hypotension...
  4. Patient requiring prompt endoscopy...

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. B (Cholelithiasis).
2. E (H. pylori causes ulcers).
3. A (IV fluid resuscitation).
4. A (New dyspepsia with weight loss).

Clinical Pearls

  • H. pylori and NSAIDs are main PUD causes.
  • H. pylori is linked to ulcers, gastric cancer, and MALT lymphoma.
  • Treatment includes acid suppression and H. pylori eradication.
  • Red flag symptoms warrant prompt endoscopy.

References

Bytzer P, et al. Ann Intern Med. 2001; Del Valle J, et al. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 2012.

Case 5: Hyponatremia, Syndrome of Inappropriate Secretion of Antidiuretic Hormone

Case Description

A 65-year-old woman with small cell lung cancer presents with confusion and lethargy. She is euvolemic, with serum sodium 108 mmol/L, serum osmolality 220 mOsm/kg, and urine osmolality 400 mOsm/kg.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the next step in therapy?
  • What are the complications of therapy?

Answers

Diagnosis: Coma/lethargy due to severe hyponatremia from SIADH.
Next step: Hypertonic saline.
Complications: Osmotic cerebral demyelination (central pontine myelinolysis).

Analysis

Objectives

  • Learn causes of hyponatremia.
  • Understand diagnostic testing for hyponatremia.
  • Know treatment and complications.

Considerations

Severe hyponatremia in a euvolemic patient with lung cancer suggests SIADH; requires careful correction to avoid complications.

Approach to Hyponatremia

Definitions

  • ADH: Hormone controlling free water excretion.
  • Osmolality: Concentration of osmotically active particles.
  • SIADH: Nonphysiologic ADH elevation.

Clinical Approach

Explains hyponatremia causes, assessment (volume status, urine osmolality), and treatments (fluid restriction, saline, vaptans). Highlights risks of rapid correction.

Comprehension Questions

  1. Cause of hyponatremia post-splenectomy...
  2. Cause of hyponatremia in a smoker with hyperkalemia...
  3. Treatment for hypovolemic hyponatremia...
  4. Laboratory findings for hypotonic solution-induced hyponatremia...

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. A (Elevated vasopressin).
2. D (Adrenal insufficiency).
3. B (0.9% saline).
4. C (Low serum osmolarity).

Clinical Pearls

  • Hyponatremia results from impaired free water excretion.
  • SIADH requires euvolemia, high urine osmolality, and normal adrenal/thyroid function.
  • Correct chronic hyponatremia slowly to avoid demyelination.

References

Androgue H, et al. N Engl J Med. 2000; Mount DB, et al. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 2012.

Case 6: Aortic Dissection, Marfan Syndrome

Case Description

A 42-year-old man with hypertension presents with sudden severe chest pain, unrelieved by nitroglycerin. He has asymmetric blood pressures, a new aortic insufficiency murmur, and a widened mediastinum on chest x-ray, suggesting aortic dissection and possible Marfan syndrome.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is your next step?

Answers

Diagnosis: Aortic dissection.
Next step: Administer IV beta-blocker and perform imaging (TEE, CT angiography, or MRI).

Analysis

Objectives

  • Learn clinical and radiographic features of aortic dissection.
  • Know risk factors for aortic dissection.
  • Understand management and surgical vs. medical treatment.
  • Learn about abdominal aortic aneurysm surveillance.

Considerations

Differentiating dissection from MI is critical to avoid harmful treatments like anticoagulation.

Approach to Aortic Aneurysm and Dissection

Definitions

  • AAA: Dilation of aorta >1.5 times normal diameter.
  • Aortic Dissection: Intimal tear creating a false lumen.

Clinical Approach

Details dissection causes (hypertension, Marfan syndrome), clinical features, imaging, and treatment (surgery for type A, medical for type B). Discusses AAA surveillance and repair indications.

Comprehension Questions

  1. Next step for chest pain with widened mediastinum...
  2. Management for ascending aorta dissection...
  3. Management for 4.5-cm AAA...
  4. Risk in a man with bicuspid aortic valve...

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. B (CT with contrast).
2. C (Surgical correction).
3. B (Serial ultrasound).
4. C (Not at increased risk for AAA).

Clinical Pearls

  • Hypertension is a major risk for aortic dissection.
  • Type A dissections require urgent surgery; type B can be managed medically.
  • AAA >5.5 cm warrants surgical repair.
  • Screen men aged 65-75 with smoking history for AAA.

References

Case 7: HIV and Pneumocystis Pneumonia

Summary

A 32-year-old man with known HIV infection presents with subacute fever, dry cough, and worsening dyspnea. He is not on antiretroviral therapy or prophylactic medications. Chest x-ray shows diffuse bilateral pulmonary infiltrates, and he is tachypneic and hypoxemic. Oral thrush suggests immunosuppression. Leukocyte count is low (<3500 cells/mm³), and LDH is elevated.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the next step?
  • What other diagnoses should be considered?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: AIDS and probable Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP).
Next step: Obtain arterial blood gas measurement to quantify hypoxemia, as it impacts treatment.
Other diagnoses: Tuberculosis, atypical mycobacteria, cryptococcosis, disseminated histoplasmosis, and community-acquired pneumonias (e.g., Streptococcus pneumoniae, Mycoplasma, influenza).

Analysis

Objectives

  • Understand the natural history of HIV infection.
  • Know opportunistic infections at various HIV immunocompromise levels.
  • Be familiar with respiratory infections in AIDS.
  • Understand indications for antiretroviral therapy and prophylactic medications.

Considerations

Subacute dyspnea, dry cough, and elevated LDH suggest PCP. Oral thrush indicates a CD4 count <250 cells/mm³, and if <200, PCP is likely. Arterial blood gas guides prognosis and therapy (e.g., corticosteroids for PO2 <70 mm Hg or A-a gradient >35 mm Hg, followed by TMP-SMX).

Approach to HIV Infections

Definitions

  • Pneumocystis jirovecii: Unicellular fungus causing pneumonia in immunocompromised patients, especially HIV with CD4 <200 cells/mm³.
  • AIDS: CD4 count <200 cells/mm³ or an AIDS-defining illness in an HIV-positive patient.

Clinical Approach

Assess CD4 count to gauge immunodeficiency. Normal CD4 is 600-1500 cells/mm³. Below 500, patients are prone to recurrent pneumonias, TB, candidiasis, and herpes zoster. Below 200, PCP, toxoplasmosis, and cryptococcosis occur. Below 50, CMV and MAC are risks. PCP diagnosis often requires sputum induction or bronchoalveolar lavage. Treatment includes TMP-SMX, with corticosteroids for severe hypoxia. Other infections (TB, cryptococcosis, toxoplasmosis, CMV, MAC) are considered based on clinical and radiographic findings. Prophylaxis (e.g., TMP-SMX for PCP at CD4 <200) and HAART are critical.

Comprehension Questions

  1. A 32-year-old woman with HIV (CD4 100 cells/mm³) has fever, shortness of breath, and dry cough. Which test confirms the diagnosis?
  2. Which organism is most likely to cause lobar pneumonia in an AIDS patient?
  3. A 44-year-old woman with HIV (CD4 180 cells/mm³) needs prophylaxis. Which is recommended?
  4. A 36-year-old woman with HIV has new-onset seizures and multiple ring-enhancing brain lesions. What is the best therapy?

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. A (Silver stain of sputum for PCP).
2. D (Streptococcus pneumoniae).
3. C (Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for PCP).
4. D (Sulfadiazine with pyrimethamine for toxoplasmosis).

Clinical Pearls

  • PCP typically presents subacutely with fever and dry cough in patients with CD4 <200 cells/mm³, often with elevated LDH.
  • Pulmonary TB in AIDS may have atypical radiographic findings.
  • Community-acquired pneumonia in AIDS is caused by typical organisms like Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • TMP-SMX prevents PCP (CD4 <200) and toxoplasmosis (CD4 <100); clarithromycin/azithromycin prevents MAC (CD4 <50).
  • HAART reduces viral replication but should not be started during acute illness due to IRIS.

References

Fauci AS, et al. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 18th ed. 2012:1506-1587.
Gray F, et al. J Neuropathol Exp Neurol. 2003;62:429-440.
Smulian AG, et al. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 18th ed. 2012:1671-1674.
Thomas CF, et al. N Engl J Med. 2004;350:2487-2498.
Wolff AJ, et al. Chest. 2001;120:1888-1893.

Case 8: Limb Ischemia (Peripheral Vascular Disease)

Summary

A 58-year-old man with chronic stable angina, hypercholesterolemia, and hypertension presents with severe left calf and foot pain, numbness, and inability to move toes. He has a history of progressive claudication, now with rest pain, pallor, and absent left pedal pulses.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the next step?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Acute limb ischemia, likely thrombotic arterial occlusion or embolism.
Next step: Angiogram of the lower extremity.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Understand the clinical presentation of atherosclerotic peripheral vascular disease (PVD) and acute limb ischemia.
  • Know the evaluation and medical management of PVD.
  • Understand indications for extremity revascularization.

Considerations

The patient has systemic atherosclerosis (angina, carotid bruit). Progressive claudication and recent rest pain suggest critical limb ischemia. Sudden pain, pallor, and pulselessness indicate acute arterial occlusion, possibly from an embolus or thrombosis, requiring urgent imaging for revascularization planning.

Approach to Peripheral Vascular Disease

Definitions

  • Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI): Ratio of ankle to brachial systolic blood pressure, measured by Doppler ultrasound.
  • Claudication: Pain or cramp in muscles during exertion, relieved by rest.

Clinical Approach

PVD affects up to 16% of Americans over 55, with smoking and diabetes as key risk factors. Claudication is the hallmark symptom, with pain distal to arterial stenosis. Physical exam may show diminished pulses, bruits, pallor on elevation, or rubor on dependency. ABI (normal >1, claudication 0.41-0.90, critical ischemia ≤0.40) confirms diagnosis. Management includes smoking cessation, exercise, and medications (e.g., cilostazol). Revascularization is indicated for critical ischemia (ABI <0.40, rest pain, nonhealing ulcers). Acute occlusion requires immediate anticoagulation and angiography.

Comprehension Questions

  1. A 49-year-old smoker with hypertension, diabetes, and hypercholesterolemia has calf pain when walking. What offers the greatest benefit?
  2. A 31-year-old male smoker has rest pain and a nonhealing foot ulcer. What is the likely cause?
  3. A 21-year-old woman with fever, fatigue, and unequal arm pulses. What is the likely cause?
  4. A 62-year-old man post-cardiac catheterization has livedo reticularis and blue toes. What is the cause?
  5. A 67-year-old woman with PVD is not a surgical candidate. What condition is likely?

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. D (Smoking cessation).
2. C (Thromboangiitis obliterans).
3. D (Takayasu aortitis).
4. A (Cholesterol embolism).
5. A (Diffuse atherosclerotic disease).

Clinical Pearls

  • Smoking cessation is the most critical intervention for PVD.
  • Revascularization is indicated for debilitating claudication, rest pain, or tissue necrosis.
  • Acute arterial occlusion (six Ps: pain, pallor, pulselessness, paresthesias, poikilothermia, paralysis) is a medical emergency.
  • Chronic incomplete occlusion causes exertional pain, pallor on elevation, and rubor on dependency.

References

Creager M, et al. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 18th ed. 2012:2066-2076.
Hankey GJ, et al. JAMA. 2006;295:547.
Hirsch AT, et al. Circulation. 2006;113:e463.
Katzen BT. Rev Cardiovasc Med. 2002;3(Suppl 2):S2-S6.

Case 9: Hypertension, Outpatient

Summary

A 56-year-old man with a history of hypertension and hypercholesterolemia presents with blood pressure of ~170/95 mm Hg. Funduscopic exam shows hypertensive retinopathy, and cardiac exam suggests cardiomegaly and left ventricular hypertrophy. He is obese, smokes, and has multiple cardiovascular risk factors.

Questions

  • What are the next steps?

Answers

Next steps:
1. Laboratory evaluation (electrolytes, creatinine, urinalysis, glucose, lipid profile, ECG) for target organ damage.
2. Start two-drug antihypertensive regimen including a thiazide diuretic.
3. Recommend lifestyle changes, especially smoking cessation.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Understand initial evaluation of hypertension.
  • Know common antihypertensive medications and their indications/cautions.
  • Be familiar with secondary hypertension causes and when to pursue them.

Considerations

Severe hypertension (stage II, >160/100 mm Hg) with end-organ damage (retinopathy, left ventricular hypertrophy) warrants immediate two-drug therapy. Essential hypertension is likely, but secondary causes (e.g., renal artery stenosis) should be considered.

Approach to Hypertension

Definitions

  • Essential Hypertension: Idiopathic hypertension (80-95% of cases).
  • Lifestyle Modification: Includes exercise, weight loss, reduced salt, and fat intake.
  • Prehypertension: BP 120-139/80-89 mm Hg.
  • Stage I Hypertension: BP 140-159/90-99 mm Hg.
  • Stage II Hypertension: BP >160/100 mm Hg.
  • Secondary Hypertension: Hypertension with a known cause (5-20% of cases).

Clinical Approach

Stage hypertension using multiple BP readings. Evaluate for secondary causes (renal, endocrine, etc.) in young patients, refractory cases, or with suggestive signs. Assess cardiovascular risk factors and target organ damage (cardiomyopathy, nephropathy, retinopathy). Start lifestyle modifications for all; stage I requires one drug, stage II requires two. Thiazides are first-line for uncomplicated hypertension per JNC 7. Tailor therapy to comorbidities (e.g., ACE inhibitors for diabetes).

Comprehension Questions

  1. A 30-year-old woman with BP 160/100 mm Hg, obesity, striae, and hirsutism. What is the likely diagnosis?
  2. A 45-year-old man with idiopathic hypertension (BP 150/100). What provides prognostic information?
  3. A 34-year-old woman with stage I hypertension and no complications. What is the first-line agent?
  4. A 45-year-old man with diabetes and BP 145/90. What is the best initial therapy?

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. C (Cushing syndrome).
2. B (End-organ effects like left ventricular hypertrophy).
3. A (Thiazide diuretics).
4. B (ACE inhibitor).

Clinical Pearls

  • Hypertension diagnosis requires two or more BP measurements on separate visits.
  • Evaluate cardiovascular risk and target organ damage.
  • Most hypertension is essential, but screen for secondary causes when indicated.
  • Renal diseases are the most common secondary hypertension causes.
  • Lifestyle modifications are key for hypertension control.
  • BP reduction magnitude is more critical than drug class for risk reduction.

References

Chobanian AV, et al. JAMA. 2003;289:2560-2572.
Kotchen TA. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 18th ed. 2012:2042-2059.

Case 10: Hypertensive Encephalopathy/Pheochromocytoma

Summary

A 39-year-old man with recent hypertension diagnosis presents with confusion, BP 215/132 mm Hg, tachycardia, diaphoresis, dilated pupils, and papilledema. He reports intermittent headaches, palpitations, and lightheadedness. He recently switched from clonidine to metoprolol. CT head and CSF studies rule out hemorrhage or infection.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the underlying etiology?
  • What is the next step?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Hypertensive encephalopathy.
Possible etiology: Pheochromocytoma, possible clonidine rebound hypertension.
Next step: Admit to ICU, lower BP with parenteral agent, and monitor arterial pressure.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Learn definitions and management of hypertensive emergencies and urgencies.
  • Understand systemic BP and cerebral blood flow relationship.
  • Know diagnosis and treatment of pheochromocytoma.

Considerations

Severe hypertension with confusion suggests hypertensive encephalopathy, a diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out stroke or infection. Urgent BP reduction is needed, but not to normal levels to avoid hypoperfusion. The hyperadrenergic state (tachycardia, diaphoresis, tremor) suggests pheochromocytoma or clonidine rebound.

Approach to Hypertensive Emergencies

Definitions

  • MEN IIA: Multiple endocrine neoplasia with pheochromocytoma, medullary thyroid cancer, and hyperparathyroidism.
  • MEN IIB: Pheochromocytoma, medullary thyroid cancer, and mucosal neuromas.

Clinical Approach

Hypertensive emergencies involve end-organ damage (e.g., encephalopathy, myocardial ischemia, aortic dissection) and require immediate BP reduction with IV medications in the ICU. Urgencies lack end-organ damage and can be managed outpatient. Cerebral autoregulation fails at high BPs, causing edema and encephalopathy. Sodium nitroprusside is commonly used, but specific therapies (e.g., beta-blockers for dissection) are tailored. Pheochromocytoma is diagnosed with urine/plasma metanephrines and treated with alpha-blockade before surgery.

Comprehension Questions

  1. A 50-year-old man with chronic hypertension, off medications, has BP 200/104 mm Hg and is asymptomatic. Best management?
  2. An 80-year-old woman post-hip surgery has BP 178/110 mm Hg and hip pain. Next step?
  3. A 61-year-old man with coronary artery disease, orthopnea, and BP 190/105 mm Hg. Next step?
  4. A 58-year-old woman with 8-hour aphasia and right-arm weakness, BP 162/98 mm Hg. Next step?

Answers to Comprehension Questions

1. B (Prescribe clonidine and recheck in 24-48 hours).
2. B (Control pain and monitor BP).
3. D (Start ACE inhibitor).
4. D (Observe BP).

Clinical Pearls

  • Hypertensive emergency involves acute end-organ damage requiring immediate BP reduction.
  • Asymptomatic high BP is managed outpatient with oral medications.
  • Chronic hypertension shifts cerebral autoregulation, risking hypoperfusion if BP is normalized rapidly.
  • Pheochromocytoma causes paroxysmal hypertension with headaches, palpitations, and diaphoresis.
  • Alpha-blockade precedes beta-blockade in pheochromocytoma to avoid unopposed alpha effects.

Neumann HP. Harrison’s Principles of Internal Medicine. 18th ed. 2012:2962-2967.

Case 11: Acute Viral Hepatitis, Possible Acetaminophen Hepatotoxicity

Summary

A 28-year-old man presents with 5 days of nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fever (101°F), and myalgias. He has 12 lifetime sexual partners and takes acetaminophen (30 tablets/day for 2 days). He is jaundiced, with tender hepatomegaly, elevated transaminases (ALT 3440 IU/L, AST 2705 IU/L), bilirubin (24.5 mg/dL), and prolonged prothrombin time (14 seconds).

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the most important immediate diagnostic test?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Acute hepatitis, possibly viral or toxic, exacerbated by acetaminophen.
Most important test: Acetaminophen level to assess toxicity risk.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Understand viral serologic studies for hepatitis A, B, and C.
  • Know prognosis for acute viral hepatitis and recognize fulminant hepatic failure.
  • Understand hepatitis A and B prevention measures.
  • Know acetaminophen nomogram and hepatotoxicity treatment.

Considerations

Acute hepatic injury with systemic symptoms predates acetaminophen use, suggesting viral hepatitis or toxic injury. Sexual history raises hepatitis B/C risk. High transaminases (>1000 IU/L) indicate significant necrosis. Acetaminophen level is critical due to treatable toxicity risk.

Approach to Suspected Hepatitis

Definitions

  • Hepatitis: Liver inflammation, often viral (A, B, C, D, E, G).

Case 23: Alcoholic Ketoacidosis

Summary

A 45-year-old man with a history of alcohol abuse is brought to the emergency room with nausea and vomiting after a prolonged drinking binge. The Acetest is weakly positive for ketones, but his serum glucose is not elevated.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is the best initial treatment?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Alcoholic ketoacidosis.
Best initial treatment: Infusion of 5% dextrose with 0.9% saline.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Understand how to diagnose and classify simple acid-base disorders.
  • Know how to calculate the anion gap (AG), and how to manage the causes of high AG acidosis.
  • Understand the two causes of nongap acidosis (bicarbonate losses from the GI tract and renal tubular acidosis).
  • Understand how to discriminate between saline-responsive and saline-resistant metabolic alkalosis.

The most significant finding in this case is an apparent acidosis with an elevated anion gap. In this patient with a history of alcoholism and an alcohol binge, alcoholic ketoacidosis is most likely, but one must also consider the possibility of other ingestions (methanol, ethylene glycol), either accidental or intentional, that would also present with similar laboratory findings, but may be more serious or even fatal.

Approach to Alcoholic Ketoacidosis

Definitions

  • Anion gap (AG): [Na⁺] – [Cl⁻ + HCO3⁻]. An estimation of unmeasured anions in the plasma (phosphate, sulfate, organic acids).
  • Osmolal gap: The difference between the measured and calculated serum osmolality. If elevated (e.g., >10 mOsm), then suggestive of the presence of significant serum concentration of an additional osmotically active solute such as methanol or ethylene glycol.
  • Urine anion gap (UAG): Urine [Na⁺ + K⁺] – urine [Cl⁻]. An estimation of urinary ammonium ion (NH4⁺) excretion.

Clinical Approach

In normal physiology, systemic pH is maintained between 7.35 and 7.45. The main homeostatic mechanisms are respiratory and renal. Disturbances in one system cause compensatory changes in the other. Simple acid-base disorders can be classified using pH, PaCO2, and HCO3⁻. For metabolic acidosis, calculate the anion gap (AG). Causes of high AG metabolic acidosis include lactic acidosis, ketoacidosis, toxins (methanol, ethylene glycol), and renal failure. In alcoholic ketoacidosis, decreased carbohydrate intake and alcohol-induced inhibition of gluconeogenesis lead to increased ketoacid formation, predominantly β-hydroxybutyrate. Treatment involves dextrose and saline to increase insulin secretion and replenish fluid deficits. Thiamine should be administered prior to glucose to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy. Suspect methanol or ethylene glycol ingestion if there is a high osmolal gap and low ethanol levels; treat with fomepizole. Non-AG acidosis may result from GI bicarbonate losses or renal tubular acidosis (RTA), assessed via urine anion gap. Respiratory acidosis and alkalosis are caused by changes in ventilation, while metabolic alkalosis involves acid loss or HCO3⁻ retention, classified as saline-responsive or saline-resistant based on urine chloride levels.

Approach to Low Back Pain

Definitions

  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: Lower back pain, saddle anesthesia, and bowel or bladder dysfunction with possible lower extremity weakness and loss of reflexes caused by compression of multiple sacral nerve roots. Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency.
  • Sciatica: Pain in the distribution of the lumbar or sacral nerve roots, with or without motor or sensory deficits.
  • Spondylolisthesis: Anterior displacement of an upper vertebral body on the lower body, which can cause symptoms and signs of spinal stenosis. This condition can result from spondylolysis or from degenerative disk disease in the elderly.
  • Spondylolysis: Defect in the pars interarticularis, either congenital or secondary to a stress fracture, leading to lower back pain, back muscle spasms, or no symptoms.

Clinical Approach

Low back pain affects two-thirds of adults at some point, most commonly between ages 30 and 60. It is recurrent, often mild in younger patients, resolving within 2 weeks, but can be more severe with age. Clinicians must exclude serious conditions like malignancy, infection, or neurologic processes (e.g., cauda equina syndrome). Most cases are idiopathic musculoskeletal low back pain, managed conservatively, with 90% resolving in 4-6 weeks. “Red flag” symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss, neurologic deficits) necessitate urgent evaluation. Imaging is reserved for persistent pain (>4-6 weeks), suspected malignancy, or infection. Treatment includes NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, and maintaining activity; bed rest is not recommended. Surgery is considered for persistent radicular pain or neurologic deficits.

Clinical Pearls

  • Acute low back pain, even with sciatic nerve involvement, resolves within 4 to 6 weeks in 90% of patients.
  • Analgesics, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs or narcotics, muscle relaxants, and attempts at maintaining some level of activity are helpful in managing acute low back pain; bed rest does not help.
  • Pain that interferes with sleep, significant unintentional weight loss, or fever suggests an infectious or neoplastic cause of back pain.
  • Imaging studies, such as magnetic resonance imaging, are useful only if surgery is being considered (persistent pain and neurologic symptoms after 4 to 6 weeks of conservative care in patients with herniated disks) or if a neoplastic or infectious cause of back pain is being considered.
  • Signs for cauda equina syndrome are a clinical emergency and require immediate referral to surgery for decompression.

Case 25: Iron-Deficiency Anemia

Summary

A healthy 52-year-old man complains of a 4- to 5-month history of increasing exercise intolerance, but he denies orthopnea, PND, edema, or other signs of heart failure. The patient uses a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) regularly. He has not had any overt gastrointestinal (GI) blood loss. On examination, he weighs 205 lb, and he has slight pallor of the conjunctiva, skin, and palms. He is anemic, with a hemoglobin level of 8.2 g/dL.

Questions

  • What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • What is your next diagnostic step?

Answers

Most likely diagnosis: Iron-deficiency anemia as a result of chronic blood loss.
Next diagnostic step: Analyze the complete blood count (CBC), particularly the mean corpuscular volume (MCV), to determine if the anemia is microcytic, normocytic, or macrocytic; assess the leukocyte count and platelet count.

Analysis

Objectives

  • Understand that iron-deficiency anemia is the most common cause of anemia.
  • Know the diagnostic approach to anemia.
  • Be familiar with the treatment of iron-deficiency anemia.

Considerations

This 52-year-old man presents with fatigue and dyspnea on exertion for a few months. His physical examination is significant only for pallor. The serum hemoglobin level confirms anemia. The next step would be to characterize the anemia as microcytic, consistent with iron deficiency, and confirm with further testing for total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) and ferritin. The most likely source of blood loss in male patients is the GI tract; therefore, finding iron-deficiency anemia should suggest a possible GI source of bleeding, with colon cancer the most serious possibility. This patient is using an NSAID, which may predispose to erosive gastritis. Once iron-deficiency anemia is confirmed, a thorough evaluation of the GI tract, including upper and lower endoscopy, is needed.

Approach to Suspected Iron-Deficiency Anemia

Definitions

  • Anemia: Decreased red blood cell (RBC) mass, leading to less oxygen-carrying capacity. Hemoglobin levels less than 13 g/dL in men and less than 12 g/dL in women are generally used.
  • Iron Studies: Ferritin is a marker of iron stores, but it also is an acute-phase reactant, decreased in iron deficiency but increased with inflammatory chronic diseases. The TIBC is an indirect measure of transferrin saturation levels and is increased in iron deficiency.
  • Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV): Average RBC volume. This offers a method of categorizing anemias as microcytic (MCV <80 fL), normocytic (MCV 80-100 fL), and macrocytic (MCV >100 fL).
  • Reticulocyte: New RBC that usually is 1 to 1.5 days old.
  • Reticulocyte Count: Fraction of RBCs consisting of reticulocytes that indirectly indicates the bone marrow activity of the erythrocyte line. It usually is expressed as a percentage and normally is 1%; corrected reticulocyte count accounts for anemia.

Clinical Approach

Iron deficiency is the most common cause of anemia in the United States, primarily due to blood loss (e.g., GI bleeding in men, menstrual loss in women). Iron is essential for hemoglobin synthesis. Initial evaluation involves CBC with MCV to classify anemia as microcytic, normocytic, or macrocytic. Iron deficiency typically presents as microcytic anemia with high RDW. Iron studies (low ferritin, high TIBC, low transferrin saturation) confirm the diagnosis. Treatment involves oral ferrous sulfate (325 mg two or three times daily) for 6 months to replenish stores, with endoscopic evaluation to identify GI bleeding sources, especially in men or postmenopausal women. Symptoms include fatigue, dyspnea, and sometimes pica or pagophagia. Severe cases may show glossitis, cheilosis, or koilonychia.

Clinical Pearls

  • Anemia is a clinical finding, not a diagnosis, and requires investigation to determine the underlying etiology.
  • Iron-deficiency anemia in men or postmenopausal women is primarily a result of gastrointestinal blood losses; therefore, finding iron-deficiency anemia in this patient population warrants a thorough gastrointestinal workup.
  • Iron-deficiency anemia in women of reproductive age is most often caused by menstrual blood loss.
  • Fecal occult blood testing is negative in approximately 50% of patients with gastrointestinal cancer. Therefore, a negative fecal occult blood test in the presence of iron-deficiency anemia should not discourage pursuing a thorough gastrointestinal workup.
  • The mean corpuscular volume, red blood cell distribution width, and reticulocyte index are important parameters in the evaluation of anemia.

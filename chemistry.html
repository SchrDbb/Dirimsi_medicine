Introduction

Chemistry is the branch of science that explores the composition, structure, properties, and transformations of matter. Often referred to as the "central science," chemistry bridges physics and biology, providing insights into the molecular basis of natural phenomena. Antoine Laurent Lavoisier, known as the father of modern chemistry, laid foundational principles through his work on the conservation of mass. This chapter introduces fundamental concepts, including the nature of matter, atomic and molecular structures, measurement techniques, laws governing chemical combinations, and the quantitative aspects of chemical reactions through stoichiometry and the mole concept.

1.1 Introduction to Chemistry and Matter

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define chemistry and its major branches
  • Classify matter into pure substances and mixtures
  • Describe methods for separating mixtures

Chemistry is the study of matter's composition, structure, and properties. It encompasses several branches:

  • Inorganic Chemistry: Focuses on elements (excluding carbon) and their compounds.
  • Organic Chemistry: Studies carbon-containing compounds, typically those from living organisms.
  • Physical Chemistry: Explores the principles governing chemical interactions.
  • Analytical Chemistry: Involves qualitative and quantitative analysis of substances.
  • Other Branches: Include biochemistry, nuclear chemistry, forensic chemistry, and more.

Matter

Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass, composed of particles that are in constant motion and attract each other. Matter is classified into:

  • Pure Substances: Have a fixed composition and distinct properties.
    • Elements: Simplest form, containing one type of atom (e.g., hydrogen, oxygen). There are 118 known elements, with hydrogen being the most abundant in the universe and oxygen (46.6%) in Earth's crust.
    • Compounds: Formed by combining elements in a definite ratio by mass (e.g., water, \(\ce{H2O}\), with hydrogen and oxygen in a 1:8 ratio).
  • Mixtures: Contain two or more substances with variable composition, classified as homogeneous (uniform composition) or heterogeneous (non-uniform composition).

Separation of Mixtures

Mixtures can be separated using physical methods:

  • Filtration: Separates solids from liquids using a filter.
  • Distillation: Separates components based on differences in boiling points.
  • Sublimation: Separates substances like iodine that convert directly from solid to vapor (e.g., \(\ce{I2}\)).
  • Crystallization: Separates solids based on solubility differences.
  • Magnetic Separation: Isolates magnetic components.
  • Atmolysis: Separates gases based on diffusion rates.

1.2 Atoms, Molecules, and Atomic Mass

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define atoms and molecules
  • Explain atomic mass and its calculation
  • Describe Dalton’s atomic theory

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that can participate in a chemical reaction, such as \(\ce{H}\), \(\ce{Cl}\), or \(\ce{O}\). A molecule is the smallest particle of a substance with independent existence, either homoatomic (e.g., \(\ce{H2}\), \(\ce{O3}\)) or heteroatomic (e.g., \(\ce{HCl}\), \(\ce{NH3}\)).

Atomic Mass

Atomic mass is the average mass of an atom relative to \(\frac{1}{12}\) the mass of a carbon-12 (\(\ce{^{12}C}\)) atom, expressed in atomic mass units (amu or u):

\[ \text{Average atomic mass} = \frac{\text{average mass of an atom}}{\frac{1}{12} \text{mass of one } \ce{^{12}C} \text{ atom}} \]

Since elements exist as mixtures of isotopes, the average atomic mass accounts for isotopic abundance:

\[ \text{Average atomic mass} = \frac{\sum (\text{relative abundance} \times \text{atomic mass of isotope})}{\sum \text{relative abundance}} \]

For example, chlorine has isotopes \(\ce{^{35}Cl}\) (75.77%, 34.97 u) and \(\ce{^{37}Cl}\) (24.23%, 36.97 u):

\[ \text{Average atomic mass of Cl} = \frac{(0.7577 \times 34.97) + (0.2423 \times 36.97)}{0.7577 + 0.2423} \approx 35.45 \, \text{u} \]

Gram Atomic Mass (GAM) is the atomic mass expressed in grams, equivalent to one mole of atoms.

Dalton’s Atomic Theory

Proposed in 1803, Dalton’s atomic theory includes:

  1. Matter consists of indivisible atoms.
  2. Atoms of an element are identical in mass and properties.
  3. Atoms cannot be created or destroyed in chemical reactions.
  4. Compounds form by combining atoms in simple numerical ratios.

Limitations: It failed to explain molecular formation and differences in atomic masses and valencies.

Smallest particle of an element that can participate in a chemical reaction.
molecule
Smallest particle of a substance with independent existence.
atomic mass
Average mass of an atom relative to \(\frac{1}{12}\) of a \(\ce{^{12}C}\) atom.
gram atomic mass
Atomic mass of an element expressed in grams.

1.3 Physical Quantities and Measurements

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Identify basic and derived units in the SI system
  • Explain scientific notation and dimensional analysis
  • Define precision and accuracy in measurements

Physical quantities, such as mass, length, and volume, are measured without chemical reactions and expressed as a numerical value multiplied by a unit:

\[ \text{Magnitude} = \text{numerical value} \times \text{unit} \]

Units

The SI system defines seven basic units: length (m), mass (kg), time (s), electric current (A), temperature (K), amount of substance (mol), and luminous intensity (Cd). Derived units, like density (\(\ce{kg \, m^{-3}}\)), are combinations of basic units.

Common SI prefixes adjust unit magnitudes:

Table 1.1 SI Prefixes
Prefix Symbol Multiple
kilo k \(10^3\)
milli m \(10^{-3}\)
micro \(\mu\) \(10^{-6}\)
nano n \(10^{-9}\)

Scientific Notation

Scientific notation expresses numbers as \( N \times 10^n \), where \( 1.000 \leq N \leq 9.999 \). For example:

  • \( 138.42 \, \text{cm} = 1.3842 \times 10^2 \, \text{cm} \)
  • \( 0.0002 = 2.0 \times 10^{-4} \)

Precision and Accuracy

Precision measures the reproducibility of measurements:

\[ \text{Precision} = |\text{individual value} - \text{arithmetic mean}| \]

Accuracy measures closeness to the true value:

\[ \text{Accuracy} = |\text{mean value} - \text{true value}| \]

Precise measurements may not be accurate if systematically biased.

Significant Figures

Significant figures indicate the certainty of a measurement. Rules include:

  1. All non-zero digits and zeros between them are significant.
  2. Leading zeros are not significant; trailing zeros after a decimal are.
  3. Exact numbers have infinite significant figures.

For calculations:

  • Addition/Subtraction: Result has the same decimal places as the term with the least.
  • Multiplication/Division: Result has the same significant figures as the least precise term.

Example: \( 2.512 + 2.2 + 5.23 = 9.942 \approx 9.9 \) (one decimal place).

Dimensional Analysis

Converts units using conversion factors:

\[ \text{Information sought} = \text{Information given} \times \text{Conversion factor} \]

Example: Convert 1 L to cm³:

\[ 1 \, \text{L} \times \frac{1000 \, \text{cm}^3}{1 \, \text{L}} = 1000 \, \text{cm}^3 \]

Key Terms

Standard unit of measurement in the International System.
scientific notation
Expression of numbers as \( N \times 10^n \).
precision
Closeness of repeated measurements to each other.
accuracy
Closeness of a measurement to the true value.
significant figures
Meaningful digits in a measured or calculated quantity.

1.4 Laws of Chemical Combinations

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Describe the laws governing chemical combinations
  • Apply these laws to predict reaction outcomes

Chemical combinations follow six fundamental laws:

  1. Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1789): The total mass of reactants equals the mass of products in a chemical reaction (excluding nuclear reactions).
  2. Law of Definite Proportions (Proust, 1799): A compound contains elements in a fixed mass ratio, regardless of its source.
  3. Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton, 1803): When two elements form multiple compounds, the masses of one element combining with a fixed mass of the other are in a simple whole-number ratio (e.g., in \(\ce{NH3}\) and \(\ce{N2H4}\), hydrogen masses are in a 3:2 ratio for a fixed nitrogen mass).
  4. Law of Reciprocal Proportions (Richter, 1792): The ratio of masses of two elements combining with a fixed mass of a third element is the same or a simple multiple of their combining ratio.
  5. Gay-Lussac’s Law of Gaseous Volumes (1808): Under similar temperature and pressure, volumes of reacting gases and gaseous products are in simple whole-number ratios.
  6. Avogadro’s Hypothesis: Equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.

Example: In \(\ce{CO}\) and \(\ce{CO2}\), the masses of oxygen combining with 12 g of carbon are 16 g and 32 g, respectively, a 1:2 ratio, illustrating the law of multiple proportions.

Key Terms

law of conservation of mass
Total mass of reactants equals total mass of products.
law of definite proportions
Compounds have a fixed composition by mass.
law of multiple proportions
Masses of one element combining with a fixed mass of another are in a simple ratio.
Avogadro’s hypothesis
Equal volumes of gases at same conditions contain equal numbers of molecules.

1.5 Stoichiometry and Mole Concept

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define the mole and Avogadro’s number
  • Calculate molecular and equivalent masses
  • Determine empirical and molecular formulas
  • Explain stoichiometry and percent yield

Mole Concept

A mole is the amount of substance containing \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} \) particles (Avogadro’s number, \( N_A \)), equal to the number of atoms in 12 g of \(\ce{^{12}C}\). At STP (273 K, 1 atm), 1 mole of a gas occupies 22.4 L.

\[ \text{Number of moles} = \frac{\text{mass (g)}}{\text{molar mass (g/mol)}} \] \[ \text{Number of molecules} = \text{number of moles} \times N_A \]

One amu = \(\frac{1}{N_A} \approx 1.66 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{g}\).

Molecular Mass

Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses in a molecule, relative to \(\frac{1}{12}\) of \(\ce{^{12}C}\):

\[ \text{Molecular mass} = \frac{\text{mass of one molecule}}{\frac{1}{12} \text{mass of } \ce{^{12}C} \text{ atom}} \]

Gram Molecular Mass is the molecular mass in grams. For ionic compounds like \(\ce{NaCl}\), the formula mass is used (e.g., 58.5 u for \(\ce{NaCl}\)). Molecular mass can also be calculated as:

\[ \text{Molecular mass} = 2 \times \text{vapour density (VD)} \]

Equivalent Mass

Equivalent mass is the mass that combines with or displaces 1.008 g of hydrogen, 8 g of oxygen, or 35.5 g of chlorine:

\[ \text{Eq. wt. of metal} = \frac{\text{wt. of metal}}{\text{wt. of } \ce{H2} \text{ displaced}} \times 1.008 \] \[ \text{Eq. wt.} = \frac{\text{atomic or molecular mass}}{n\text{-factor}} \]

The \( n \)-factor depends on the reaction (e.g., basicity for acids, acidity for bases, or change in oxidation number for redox reactions).

Empirical and Molecular Formulas

The empirical formula gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (e.g., \(\ce{CH}\) for benzene). The molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms (e.g., \(\ce{C6H6}\)):

\[ \text{Molecular formula} = (\text{Empirical formula})_n, \quad n = \frac{\text{molecular mass}}{\text{empirical formula mass}} \]

Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry involves the proportions of reactants and products in a chemical reaction. The limiting reagent is completely consumed, determining the amount of product formed, while the excess reagent remains unreacted.

Percent Yield

Percent yield compares actual to theoretical yield:

\[ \text{Percent yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100 \]

Example: For \(\ce{2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O}\), if 4 g of \(\ce{H2}\) reacts with excess \(\ce{O2}\), calculate the moles of \(\ce{H2O}\) produced:

\[ \text{Moles of } \ce{H2} = \frac{4}{2.016} \approx 1.984 \, \text{mol} \] \[ \text{Moles of } \ce{H2O} = 1.984 \times \frac{2}{2} = 1.984 \, \text{mol} \]

Key Terms

mole
Amount of substance containing \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} \) particles.
molecular mass
Sum of atomic masses in a molecule, relative to \(\frac{1}{12}\) of \(\ce{^{12}C}\).
equivalent mass
Mass that reacts with 1.008 g \(\ce{H}\), 8 g \(\ce{O}\), or 35.5 g \(\ce{Cl}\).
empirical formula
Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.
stoichiometry
Study of reactant and product proportions in a chemical reaction.
percent yield
Ratio of actual to theoretical yield, expressed as a percentage.

Introduction

The concept of the atom as the fundamental building block of matter has evolved significantly since John Dalton’s proposal in 1808. Atomic structure encompasses the study of subatomic particles—electrons, protons, and neutrons—and their arrangement within the atom. This chapter explores the discovery of these particles, early atomic models like Thomson’s and Rutherford’s, the role of electromagnetic radiation in understanding atomic spectra, Bohr’s model for hydrogen-like atoms, and the quantum mechanical model that describes electron behavior with quantum numbers and orbitals.

2.1 Subatomic Particles

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Identify the properties of electrons, protons, and neutrons
  • Explain the charge-to-mass ratio of electrons
  • Describe other subatomic particles

An atom is the smallest indivisible particle of an element, with typical radii of approximately \(10^{-8} \, \text{cm}\). It consists of three primary subatomic particles: electrons, protons, and neutrons.

Electron

Discovered by J.J. Thomson through cathode ray experiments, electrons carry a negative charge of \(-1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}\) and have a mass of \(9.11 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}\). One mole of electrons weighs approximately 0.55 mg. Cathode rays exhibit:

  • Straight-line travel, producing fluorescence on glass.
  • Mechanical motion in a pinwheel.
  • Production of X-rays upon striking a metal.
  • Deflection by electric and magnetic fields.

The charge-to-mass ratio (\(e/m_e\)) of an electron, determined by Thomson in 1897, is:

\[ \frac{e}{m_e} = 1.758820 \times 10^{11} \, \text{C kg}^{-1} \]

This ratio depends on the electron’s deflection in electric and magnetic fields, influenced by charge magnitude and particle mass.

Proton

Discovered by Rutherford via anode ray experiments, protons carry a positive charge of \(+1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}\) and have a mass of 1.007276 u. The charge-to-mass ratio for protons is:

\[ \frac{e}{m_p} = 9.58 \times 10^7 \, \text{C kg}^{-1} \]

Anode rays, heavier than electrons, travel in straight lines, originate between electrodes, and are deflected by electric and magnetic fields. The \(e/m\) ratio is highest for hydrogen gas.

Neutron

  • Mesons: Unstable particles like \(\pi^+\), \(\pi^-\), \(\pi^0\) (Yukawa, 1935).
  • Anti-proton: Negative proton (Segre and Wiegland, 1955).

Thomson’s Atomic Model

J.J. Thomson proposed the "plum pudding" model, where the atom is a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded within, ensuring neutrality. This model failed to explain Rutherford’s scattering experiment results.

Rutherford’s Nuclear Model

Based on \(\alpha\)-particle scattering, Rutherford’s model (1911) proposed:

  • Most of the atom’s volume is empty space.
  • A dense, positively charged nucleus (\(\approx 10^{-13} \, \text{cm}\)) contains most of the mass.
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus in circular paths, held by electrostatic forces.

Limitations:

  • Electrons in circular orbits should emit radiation and spiral into the nucleus, making atoms unstable.
  • It does not explain electron distribution around the nucleus.

Atomic and Mass Numbers

The atomic number (\(Z\)) is the number of protons (or electrons in a neutral atom). The mass number (\(A\)) is the total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons):

\[ A = Z + \text{number of neutrons} \]

An atom is represented as \(\ce{^A_Z X}\), e.g., \(\ce{^{12}_6 C}\).

Atomic Species

  • Isotopes: Same \(Z\), different \(A\) (e.g., \(\ce{^1_1 H}\), \(\ce{^2_1 H}\)).
  • Isobars: Same \(A\), different \(Z\) (e.g., \(\ce{^{40}_{18} Ar}\), \(\ce{^{40}_{19} K}\)).
  • Isotones: Same number of neutrons (e.g., \(\ce{^3_1 H}\), \(\ce{^4_2 He}\)).
  • Isodiaphers: Same isotopic number (\(A - 2Z\)) (e.g., \(\ce{^{39}_{19} K}\), \(\ce{^{19}_9 F}\)).
  • Isoelectronic: Same number of electrons (e.g., \(\ce{Na+}\), \(\ce{Mg^{2+}}\)).
  • Isosters: Same number of atoms and electrons (e.g., \(\ce{N2}\), \(\ce{CO}\)).

2.3 Electromagnetic Radiation and Atomic Spectra

  • Explain Planck’s quantum theory and the photoelectric effect

Electromagnetic Radiation

Maxwell’s electromagnetic wave theory describes energy emitted as waves with electric and magnetic fields, traveling at the speed of light (\(c = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\)). Key properties include:

  • Wavelength (\(\lambda\)): Distance between successive crests.
  • Frequency (\(\nu\)): Number of waves per second.
  • Wave number (\(\bar{\nu}\)): Reciprocal of wavelength (\(\bar{\nu} = \frac{1}{\lambda}\), in \(\text{cm}^{-1}\)).
  • Amplitude: Height of the wave crest.

The relationship is:

\[ c = \nu \lambda \]

The electromagnetic spectrum ranges from gamma rays (\(\lambda \approx 0.01 \, \text{Å}\)) to radio waves (\(\lambda \approx 3 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Å}\)).

Planck’s Quantum Theory

Planck proposed that energy is emitted or absorbed in discrete quanta:

\[ E = h \nu = \frac{h c}{\lambda} \]

where \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J s}\) is Planck’s constant. Total energy for \(n\) quanta is:

\[ E_T = n h \nu \]

This explains phenomena like black body radiation and the photoelectric effect, where electromagnetic waves exhibit particle-like behavior.

Photoelectric Effect

Discovered by Hertz, the photoelectric effect occurs when light of sufficient frequency (\(\nu \geq \nu_0\)) ejects electrons from a metal surface:

\[ E = h \nu = h \nu_0 + \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \]

where \(\nu_0\) is the threshold frequency, \(h \nu_0\) is the work function, and \(\frac{1}{2} m v^2\) is the kinetic energy of the ejected electron.

Atomic Spectra

Atomic spectra result from electrons transitioning between energy levels, producing emission (energy emitted) or absorption (energy absorbed) spectra. Spectra are classified as:

  • Continuous: No sharp boundaries between wavelengths.
  • Line: Distinct lines at specific wavelengths, characteristic of elements like hydrogen.

2.4 Bohr’s Model of the Atom

Bohr’s Model

Proposed by Niels Bohr in 1913, this model applies to one-electron systems (\(\ce{H}\), \(\ce{He+}\), \(\ce{Li^{2+}}\)). Assumptions include:

  1. Electrons revolve in fixed, stationary orbits without energy loss.
  2. Angular momentum is quantized:
    3. \[ m v r = \frac{n h}{2 \pi}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \]
  3. Energy is emitted or absorbed during electron transitions:
    4. \[ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = h \nu = \frac{h c}{\lambda} \]
  4. The ground state is the most stable.

Orbit Properties

For the \(n\)-th orbit of an atom with atomic number \(Z\):

  • Velocity: \( v_n = \frac{2.165 \times 10^6 Z}{n} \, \text{m/s} \)
  • Radius: \( r_n = 0.53 \times \frac{n^2}{Z} \, \text{Å} \)
  • Energy: \( E_n = -\frac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2} \, \text{eV/atom} \)

Energy difference between orbits:

\[ \Delta E = 2.178 \times 10^{-18} Z^2 \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \, \text{J/atom} \]

Hydrogen Emission Spectrum

When electrons transition from higher (\(n_2\)) to lower (\(n_1\)) energy levels, they emit photons, producing line spectra. The wave number is:

\[ \bar{\nu} = \frac{1}{\lambda} = R Z^2 \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

where \(R = 109677.8 \, \text{cm}^{-1}\) is the Rydberg constant. Hydrogen spectral series include:

Table 2.1 Hydrogen Spectral Series
Series Region \(n_1\) \(n_2\)
Lyman UV 1 2, 3, 4, …
Balmer Visible 2 3, 4, 5, …
Paschen IR 3 4, 5, 6, …

Sommerfeld Extension

Sommerfeld introduced elliptical orbits, with angular momentum:

\[ m v r = \frac{k h}{2 \pi}, \quad k = 1, 2, \ldots, n \]

For higher shells (\(n \geq 2\)), orbits can be circular (\(k = n\)) or elliptical (\(k < n\)).

2.5 Quantum Mechanical Model and Electronic Configuration

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

de-Broglie Principle

de-Broglie proposed that electrons exhibit both particle and wave properties:

\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{m v} = \frac{h}{p} \]

where \(\lambda\) is the wavelength, \(p = m v\) is momentum, and \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J s}\). For kinetic energy (\(KE\)):

\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 m KE}} \]

Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle

It states that the position (\(\Delta x\)) and momentum (\(\Delta p\)) of a particle cannot be simultaneously known with precision:

\[ \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4 \pi} \]

Quantum Mechanical Model

Developed by Heisenberg and Schrödinger, this model uses the wave function (\(\psi\)) to describe electron probability. The Schrödinger equation is:

\[ \hat{H} \psi = E \psi \]

where \(\hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{U}\) is the Hamiltonian (kinetic + potential energy operators). The probability density is \(\psi^2\), and orbitals are regions where electron probability is high.

Orbitals and Their Shapes

Orbitals are described by:

  • s-orbital: Spherical, one per subshell.
  • p-orbital: Dumbbell-shaped, three per subshell (\(p_x, p_y, p_z\)).
  • d-orbital: Double-dumbbell, five per subshell (\(d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}, d_{x^2-y^2}, d_{z^2}\)).
  • f-orbital: Complex, seven per subshell.

Nodes: Regions of zero electron probability, including radial (\(n - l - 1\)) and angular (\(l\)) nodes. Total nodes = \(n - 1\).

Quantum Numbers

  • Principal (\(n\)): Shell number (\(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\)).
  • Azimuthal (\(l\)): Subshell type (\(l = 0\) to \(n-1\); \(s: l=0\), \(p: l=1\), \(d: l=2\), \(f: l=3\)).
  • Magnetic (\(m_l\)): Orbital orientation (\(m_l = -l\) to \(+l\)).
  • Spin (\(m_s\)): Electron spin (\(+1/2\) or \(-1/2\)).

Number of orbitals per subshell = \(2l + 1\); electrons per shell = \(2n^2\).

Electronic Configuration

Electrons fill orbitals according to:

  • Aufbau Principle: Lowest energy orbitals filled first, based on \(n + l\).
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons have identical quantum numbers.
  • Hund’s Rule: Orbitals in a subshell are singly occupied with parallel spins before pairing.

Order of filling: \(1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, \ldots\). Example: Chlorine (\(Z=17\)):

\[ \ce{Cl}: 1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^5 \]

Ions: Add/remove electrons from the outermost shell, e.g., \(\ce{O^{2-}}\) (from \(\ce{O}: 1s^2 2s^2 2p^4\)) is \(1s^2 2s^2 2p^6\).

3.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

Chemical bonds are forces that hold atoms together in molecules or compounds, classified as:

  • Ionic Bonds: Formed by electron transfer between a metal and a non-metal, creating oppositely charged ions (e.g., \(\ce{NaCl}\)).
  • Covalent Bonds: Involve electron sharing between non-metals (e.g., \(\ce{H2O}\)).
  • Metallic Bonds: Occur in metals, with delocalized electrons forming a "sea" around metal cations (e.g., \(\ce{Cu}\)).

Electronegativity

Electronegativity measures an atom’s ability to attract shared electrons, quantified on the Pauling scale. The difference in electronegativity (\(\Delta EN\)) determines bond type:

  • \(\Delta EN > 1.7\): Ionic
  • \(\Delta EN = 0.4–1.7\): Polar covalent
  • \(\Delta EN < 0.4\): Non-polar covalent

Example: In \(\ce{HCl}\), \(\Delta EN \approx 0.9\), indicating a polar covalent bond.

Bond Polarity and Dipole Moment

Polar covalent bonds result in a dipole moment (\(\mu\)), measured in Debye (D):

\[ \mu = q \cdot d \]

where \(q\) is the charge separation and \(d\) is the bond length. Non-polar molecules like \(\ce{O2}\) have \(\mu = 0\), while \(\ce{HCl}\) has a non-zero dipole moment.

Valence Bond Theory

Valence bond theory (VBT) explains bonding as the overlap of atomic orbitals, forming a covalent bond. Electrons in overlapping orbitals are shared, stabilizing the molecule. For example, in \(\ce{H2}\), two 1s orbitals overlap.

Hybridization

Hybridization involves mixing atomic orbitals to form hybrid orbitals with specific geometries:

  • sp: Linear, 180° (e.g., \(\ce{BeCl2}\)).
  • sp²: Trigonal planar, 120° (e.g., \(\ce{BF3}\)).
  • sp³: Tetrahedral, 109.5° (e.g., \(\ce{CH4}\)).
  • sp³d: Trigonal bipyramidal, 120°/90° (e.g., \(\ce{PCl5}\)).
  • sp³d²: Octahedral, 90° (e.g., \(\ce{SF6}\)).

The number of hybrid orbitals equals the number of atomic orbitals mixed.

Sigma and Pi Bonds

Bonds are classified as:

  • Sigma (\(\sigma\)): Formed by head-on orbital overlap, single bonds.
  • Pi (\(\pi\)): Formed by sideways overlap, found in double/triple bonds (e.g., \(\ce{C2H4}\) has one \(\sigma\) and one \(\pi\) bond per double bond).

Molecular Orbital Theory

Molecular orbital theory (MOT) describes electrons in molecular orbitals formed by the combination of atomic orbitals. Orbitals are:

Bond Order

Bond order indicates bond strength:

\[ \text{Bond order} = \frac{\text{Number of bonding electrons} - \text{Number of antibonding electrons}}{2} \]

\[ \text{Bond order} = \frac{8 - 4}{2} = 2 \]

Types of Intermolecular Forces

The Kossel-Lewis approach states that atoms form bonds to complete their octet, acquiring the electron configuration of the nearest noble gas (octet rule). This is achieved by gaining, losing, or sharing electrons.

Octet Rule

4.2 Ionic Bonding

Ionic Bond Formation

An ionic bond forms by the complete transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal, creating ions with noble gas configurations. For example, in sodium chloride:

\[ \ce{Na (2,8,1) + Cl (2,8,7) -> Na+ (2,8) + Cl- (2,8,8)} \]

Favorable Factors for Ionic Bonds

  • Low ionization enthalpy of the metal.
  • High electron gain enthalpy of the non-metal.
  • High lattice enthalpy (energy released during crystal lattice formation).

Some elements show variable electrovalency due to unstable penultimate orbits or the inert pair effect.

Ions

Ions are species with a positive (cations, usually metals) or negative (anions, usually non-metals except H) charge.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

  • Solid at room temperature.
  • High melting and boiling points.
  • Soluble in polar solvents (e.g., water) but insoluble in non-polar solvents (e.g., benzene, \(\ce{CCl4}\)).
  • Conduct electricity in molten or aqueous states.
  • Have crystalline structures.

Writing Formulas for Ionic Compounds

To write the formula:

  1. Place the cation symbol on the left and the anion on the right.
  2. Note their electrovalencies, e.g., \(\ce{A^x B^y}\).
  3. Divide by the highest common factor (HCF).
  4. Apply the criss-cross rule: \(\ce{A_y B_x}\).

Example: Aluminum sulfate: \(\ce{Al^3+ SO4^2- -> Al2(SO4)3}\).

Born-Haber Cycle

The Born-Haber cycle describes the formation of ionic compounds, e.g., \(\ce{NaCl}\):

\[ \ce{Na(s) + 1/2 Cl2(g) ->[Q] Na+ + Cl- ->[-U] NaCl} \]

Where:

  • \(S\): Enthalpy of sublimation
  • \(I\): Ionization enthalpy
  • \(D\): Enthalpy of dissociation
  • \(E\): Electron gain enthalpy
  • \(U\): Lattice enthalpy
  • \(Q = S + I + \frac{1}{2}D - E - U\): Total enthalpy change

4.3 Covalent Bonding

Covalent Bond Formation

A covalent bond forms by mutual sharing of electrons between atoms to complete their octets or duplets. The number of shared electrons is the covalency. Example: Chlorine molecule (\(\ce{Cl2}\)):

\[ \ce{Cl (2,8,7) + Cl (2,8,7) -> Cl-Cl} \]

Shared electron pairs are bond pairs, while non-bonding pairs are lone pairs.

Types of Covalent Bonds

  • Non-polar Covalent Bond: Formed between atoms with equal electronegativity, e.g., \(\ce{H2}\), \(\ce{Cl2}\).
  • Polar Covalent Bond: Formed between atoms with different electronegativities, developing partial ionic character, e.g., \(\ce{HCl}\).

Properties of Covalent Compounds

  • Exist as liquids or gases at room temperature due to weak intermolecular forces.
  • Low melting and boiling points.
  • Poor conductors of electricity (no free electrons or ions).
  • Soluble in non-polar solvents (e.g., benzene) but insoluble in water.

Formal Charge

The formal charge (F.C.) on an atom in a molecule is:

\[ \text{F.C.} = [\text{Valence electrons}] - [\text{Non-bonding electrons}] - \frac{1}{2}[\text{Bonding electrons}] \]

Example: In ozone (\(\ce{O3}\)):

  • F.C. on O (double bonded): \(6 - 2 - \frac{1}{2}(6) = +1\)
  • F.C. on O (central): \(6 - 4 - \frac{1}{2}(4) = 0\)
  • F.C. on O (single bonded): \(6 - 6 - \frac{1}{2}(2) = -1\)

Bond Characteristics

  • Bond Length: Distance between nuclei of bonded atoms, e.g., \(\ce{C-H}\): 107 pm, \(\ce{C=C}\): 133 pm. Determined by X-ray or electron diffraction.
  • Bond Angle: Angle between bonds, affected by lone pairs and hybridization, e.g., \(\ce{CH4}\): 109.5°.
  • Bond Order: Number of covalent bonds, calculated as:
    • \[ \text{Bond order} = \frac{\text{Bonding electrons} - \text{Antibonding electrons}}{2} \]
  • Bond Enthalpy: Energy released/required to form/break one mole of bonds. Increases with bond order, decreases with bond length.

Fajan’s Rule

Ionic bonds develop partial covalent character when:

  • The cation is small, and the anion is large.
  • The charge on the cation or anion is high.

Molecular Orbital Theory

Molecular orbital theory (MOT) describes electrons in molecular orbitals formed by combining atomic orbitals. Bonding orbitals are lower in energy, while antibonding orbitals are higher. The linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO) forms:

\[ \psi_{\text{MO}} = \psi_A \pm \psi_B \]

Examples include \(\sigma_{1s}\), \(\sigma^*_{1s}\), \(\pi_{2p}\), and \(\pi^*_{2p}\). The energy order for homonuclear diatomic molecules (up to 14 electrons) is:

\[ \sigma_{1s} < \sigma^*_{1s} < \sigma_{2s} < \sigma^*_{2s} < \pi_{2p_x} \approx \pi_{2p_y} < \sigma_{2p_z} < \pi^*_{2p_x} \approx \pi^*_{2p_y} < \sigma^*_{2p_z} \]

For molecules with more than 14 electrons (e.g., \(\ce{O2}\), \(\ce{F2}\)):

\[ \sigma_{1s} < \sigma^*_{1s} < \sigma_{2s} < \sigma^*_{2s} < \sigma_{2p_z} < \pi_{2p_x} \approx \pi_{2p_y} < \pi^*_{2p_x} \approx \pi^*_{2p_y} < \sigma^*_{2p_z} \]

Bond Order:

\[ \text{Bond order} = \frac{N_b - N_a}{2} \]

Positive bond order indicates stability; zero or negative bond order indicates instability. Paramagnetic molecules have unpaired electrons; diamagnetic molecules have paired electrons.

Hydrogen Bonding

A hydrogen bond is an electrostatic attraction between a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, F) and another electronegative atom. Strength order:

\[ \ce{H...F} (10 \, \text{kcal/mol}) > \ce{H...O} (7 \, \text{kcal/mol}) > \ce{H...N} (2 \, \text{kcal/mol}) \]

Types:

  • Intermolecular: Between different molecules, e.g., o-nitrophenol.
  • Intramolecular: Within the same molecule, e.g., p-nitrophenol.

Applications of Hydrogen Bonding

  • Water’s Properties: High melting/boiling points due to intermolecular H-bonding in \(\ce{H2O}\).
  • Ice Density: Ice has lower density than water due to a cage-like tetrahedral structure from H-bonding.
  • Alcohols: Higher boiling points due to H-bonding.
  • Nitrophenols: o-Nitrophenol is more volatile (b.p. 214°C) than m-nitrophenol (290°C) or p-nitrophenol (279°C) due to intramolecular H-bonding (chelation).

Matter exists in five states: solid, liquid, gas, plasma, and Bose-Einstein condensate. Solids, liquids, and gases are commonly observed, while plasma and Bose-Einstein condensate require specific conditions.

Interconversion of States

States of matter are interconvertible through processes such as:

  • Melting Point: Temperature at which a solid converts to a liquid. Impurities lower the melting point. Example: Water melts at 0°C.
  • Boiling Point: Temperature at which a liquid’s vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure. Impurities and higher pressure increase the boiling point. Example: Water boils at 100°C at 1 atm.
  • Freezing Point: Temperature at which a liquid solidifies. Example: Water freezes at 0°C.
  • Evaporation: Conversion of a liquid to vapor at any temperature, causing cooling effects (e.g., water in earthen pots cools in summer, desert coolers work better on hot dry days).
  • Sublimation: Direct conversion from solid to gas.
  • Deposition: Direct conversion from gas to solid.
  • Vaporization: Conversion from liquid to gas.
  • Condensation: Conversion from gas to liquid.

Triple Point

The triple point is the temperature and pressure at which all three states (solid, liquid, gas) coexist in equilibrium. For water: 0.0098°C and 4.58 mmHg, where \(\ce{ice <=> liquid water <=> water vapor}\).

5.3 Intermolecular Forces and Thermal Energy

Intermolecular Forces

Intermolecular forces are attractive forces between molecules, known as van der Waals forces, influencing melting and boiling points. Types include:

  • London Dispersion Forces: Weakest forces, present in non-polar molecules (e.g., \(\ce{Cl2}\)). Increase with molecular size and number of electrons.
  • Dipole-Dipole Interactions: Between polar molecules with permanent dipoles (e.g., \(\ce{HCl}\)). Stronger than London forces, weakened by temperature increase.
  • Dipole-Induced Dipole Forces: Between polar and non-polar molecules, where a permanent dipole induces a dipole in a non-polar molecule.
  • Hydrogen Bonding: A strong dipole-dipole interaction involving N–H, O–H, or F–H bonds, driven by coulombic interactions.

Thermal Energy

Thermal energy arises from the motion of atoms or molecules, proportional to temperature (\(\text{Thermal energy} \propto T\)).

Factors Deciding Physical State

  • Gaseous State: Intermolecular forces << Thermal energy.
  • Liquid State: Intermolecular forces > Thermal energy.
  • Solid State: Intermolecular forces >> Thermal energy.

5.4 The Gaseous State and Gas Laws

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Describe characteristics of the gaseous state
  • Explain key gas laws and their applications

Characteristics of Gases

The gaseous state is the most disordered, with:

  • Weakest intermolecular forces.
  • High compressibility.
  • Equal pressure in all directions.
  • Low density compared to solids and liquids.
  • Indefinite volume and shape.
  • Complete mixing without mechanical aid.

Measurable Properties

  • Mass: Expressed in grams or kilograms.
  • Volume: Equals container volume, in liters (L), milliliters (mL), or cubic meters (\(\ce{m^3}\)). \(1 \, \text{L} = 1000 \, \text{mL} = 1000 \, \text{cm}^3 = 1 \, \text{dm}^3\).
  • Pressure: Measured with a manometer (gas) or barometer (atmospheric). Units: \(1 \, \text{atm} = 760 \, \text{mmHg} = 101.325 \, \text{kPa} = 1.01325 \, \text{bar}\).
  • Temperature: In Celsius (°C) or Kelvin (K). \(T(\text{K}) = t(\text{°C}) + 273\). STP: 273.15 K, 1 bar, molar volume = 22.71098 L/mol.

Gas Laws

  • Boyle’s Law: At constant temperature, \(V \propto \frac{1}{p}\), or \(pV = \text{constant}\). \(p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2\).
  • Charles’ Law: At constant pressure, \(V \propto T\), or \(\frac{V}{T} = \text{constant}\). \(V = V_0 \left(1 + \frac{t}{273}\right)\).
  • Gay-Lussac’s Law: At constant volume, \(p \propto T\), or \(\frac{p}{T} = \text{constant}\). \(p = p_0 \left(1 + \frac{t}{273}\right)\).
  • Avogadro’s Law: At constant T and p, \(V \propto n\), or \(\frac{V}{n} = \text{constant}\).
  • Ideal Gas Equation: \(pV = nRT\), where \(R = 0.0821 \, \text{L atm mol}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}\), \(8.314 \, \text{J mol}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}\), or \(2 \, \text{cal mol}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}\). Density: \(d = \frac{pM}{RT}\).
  • Graham’s Law of Diffusion: \(r \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\).
  • Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressure: \(p = p_1 + p_2 + \cdots\), where partial pressure = mole fraction × total pressure.

Kinetic Theory of Gases

Assumptions include negligible molecular volume, elastic collisions, no intermolecular forces, and pressure from molecular collisions. Average kinetic energy: \(\text{KE} = \frac{3}{2}RT\), per molecule: \(\frac{3}{2}kT\), where \(k = \frac{R}{N_A} = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{J K}^{-1}\).

Gas velocities:

  • Most probable velocity: \(\alpha = \sqrt{\frac{2RT}{M}}\).
  • Average velocity: \(\nu = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi M}}\).
  • Root mean square velocity: \(U = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}}\).

Ratio: \(\alpha : \nu : U = 1 : 1.128 : 1.224\).

Deviations from Ideal Behavior

Real gases deviate from \(pV = nRT\) at high pressure and low temperature. Compressibility factor: \(Z = \frac{pV}{nRT}\). \(Z = 1\) for ideal gases; \(Z < 1\) (negative deviation, more compressible); \(Z > 1\) (positive deviation, less compressible).

van der Waals’ Equation: For n moles:

\[ \left(p + \frac{n^2 a}{V^2}\right)(V - nb) = nRT \]

Where \(a\) = attractive forces, \(b = 4 \times \text{actual volume of gas molecules}\).

Charles’ law

\(pV = nRT\), relating pressure, volume, moles, and temperature.

5.5 Liquid State and Its Properties

Liquid State

Liquids have a melting point below room temperature and a boiling point above room temperature, with molecular motion intermediate between solids and gases.

Properties of Liquids

  • Vapor Pressure: Pressure exerted by vapors in equilibrium with the liquid, increasing with temperature and depending on the liquid’s nature.
  • Boiling Point: Temperature where vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure. Normal boiling point: at 1 atm; standard boiling point: at 1 bar.
  • Surface Tension (\(\gamma\)): Force per unit length perpendicular to a liquid’s surface, measured in \(\text{N m}^{-1}\). Decreases with temperature, causes capillary action, measured by stalagmometer.
  • Viscosity (\(\eta\)): Resistance to flow due to internal friction. For laminar flow: \(F = \eta A \frac{dv}{dz}\). SI unit: \(\text{N s m}^{-2}\); CGS unit: poise.

Liquefaction of Gases

Liquefaction occurs by increasing pressure or decreasing temperature, enhancing intermolecular forces. Critical points include:

  • Critical Temperature (\(T_C\)): Temperature above which a gas cannot be liquefied. \(T_C = \frac{8a}{27Rb}\). Example: \(\ce{CO2}\), 30.98°C.
  • Critical Pressure (\(p_C\)): Pressure needed to liquefy a gas at \(T_C\). \(p_C = \frac{a}{27b^2}\).
  • Critical Volume (\(V_C\)): Volume at \(T_C\) and \(p_C\). \(V_C = 3b\).
  • Boyle’s Temperature (\(T_b\)): Temperature where a real gas behaves ideally. \(T_b = \frac{a}{bR}\).

Close Packing

Close packing maximizes space efficiency in crystal structures.

  • One-Dimensional: Particles touch along a line, coordination number = 2.
  • Two-Dimensional:
    • Square Close Packing: Rows stacked directly over each other, coordination number = 4, 52.4% space occupied (AAA type).
    • Hexagonal Close Packing: Second row in depressions of first row, coordination number = 6, 60.4% space occupied (ABAB type).
  • Three-Dimensional:
    • Hexagonal Close Packing (hcp): ABAB pattern, covers tetrahedral voids, 74% space occupied, coordination number = 12.
    • Cubic Close Packing (ccp): ABCABC pattern, covers octahedral voids, 74% space occupied, coordination number = 12.
    • Body-Centred Cubic (bcc): 68% space occupied, coordination number = 8.

Order of packing efficiency: fcc > bcc > simple cubic. Noble gases have ccp structure, except \(\ce{He}\) (hcp).

Voids

Voids are empty spaces in a lattice:

  • Trigonal Voids: In 2D arrangements.
  • Tetrahedral Voids: Surrounded by four spheres, coordination number = 4, radius ratio \(r_{\text{void}}/r_{\text{sphere}} = 0.225\).
  • Octahedral Voids: Surrounded by six spheres, coordination number = 6, radius ratio \(r_{\text{void}}/r_{\text{sphere}} = 0.414\).

Number of voids: octahedral = number of close-packed particles; tetrahedral = twice the number of close-packed particles.

Packing Efficiency

Packing efficiency is the percentage of space filled by particles:

  • Simple Cubic: \(r = \frac{a}{2}\), packing efficiency = \(\frac{\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3}{a^3} = 52.4\%\).
  • Face-Centred Cubic (fcc): \(r = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{2}}\), packing efficiency = \(\frac{4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3}{(2\sqrt{2}r)^3} = 74\%\).
  • Body-Centred Cubic (bcc): \(r = \frac{\sqrt{3}a}{4}\), packing efficiency = \(\frac{2 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3}{(\frac{4r}{\sqrt{3}})^3} = 68\%\).

Imperfections in Solids

Defects are deviations from the ideal crystal structure, caused by rapid cooling or impurities. Types include:

  • Line Defects: Irregularities in entire rows of lattice points.
  • Point Defects: Irregularities around a single point or atom, classified as:
    • Stoichiometric Defects: Maintain stoichiometry.
      • Vacancy Defect: Missing lattice particles, decreases density.
      • Interstitial Defect: Particles in interstitial sites, increases density.
      • Schottky Defect: Equal number of missing cations and anions, decreases density (e.g., \(\ce{NaCl}\)).

    • Non-Stoichiometric Defects: Violate constant proportions.
      • Metal Excess: Anionic vacancies (F-centres, e.g., \(\ce{NaCl}\)) or extra cations (e.g., \(\ce{ZnO}\)).
      • Metal Deficiency: Cation vacancies balanced by higher-valence ions (e.g., \(\ce{FeO}\)).

7.1 Thermodynamic Systems and Properties

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define key thermodynamic terms
  • Differentiate types of systems and processes
  • Distinguish between state and path functions

Thermodynamics studies the relationships between heat and other energy forms. Key terms include:

  • System: The part of the universe under study.
  • Surroundings: Everything outside the system.
  • Boundary: The interface separating system and surroundings.
  • Thermodynamic Equilibrium: No change in macroscopic properties over time.
  • Thermal Equilibrium: No heat flow within the system.
  • Mechanical Equilibrium: Constant pressure, no mechanical work within the system.

Types of Systems

  • Open System: Exchanges both matter and energy with surroundings.
  • Closed System: Exchanges only energy with surroundings.
  • Isolated System: Exchanges neither matter nor energy.

Thermodynamic Properties

  • State of System: Defined by specific values of microscopic properties.
  • State Functions: Depend only on initial and final states (e.g., \(\Delta U\), \(\Delta H\), \(\Delta G\)).
  • Path Functions: Depend on the path taken (e.g., work, heat).
  • Intensive Properties: Independent of quantity (e.g., pressure, temperature).
  • Extensive Properties: Depend on quantity (e.g., internal energy, volume, enthalpy).

Thermodynamic Processes

  • Isothermal: Constant temperature, \(\Delta T = 0\), \(\Delta U = 0\).
  • Isochoric: Constant volume, \(\Delta V = 0\).
  • Isobaric: Constant pressure, \(\Delta p = 0\).
  • Adiabatic: No heat exchange, \(\Delta q = 0\).
  • Cyclic: System returns to initial state, \(\Delta U_{\text{cyclic}} = 0\), \(\Delta H_{\text{cyclic}} = 0\).
  • Reversible: Proceeds via infinitesimal steps maintaining equilibrium.
  • Irreversible: Cannot be reversed without increasing energy; all natural processes are irreversible.

7.2 First Law of Thermodynamics

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain the first law and internal energy
  • Describe modes of energy transfer (work and heat)
  • Calculate heat capacity and work

First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted between forms:

\[ \Delta U = q + W \]

Where \(\Delta U\) is the change in internal energy, \(q\) is heat added, and \(W\) is work done on the system. Sign conventions: \(q > 0\) (heat added), \(q < 0\) (heat lost), \(W > 0\) (work on system), \(W < 0\) (work by system).

Internal Energy (\(U\))

Internal energy is the total energy within a substance (vibrational, translational, etc.), an extensive state function. Absolute \(U\) is unknown, but:

\[ \Delta U = U_2 - U_1 = \Sigma U_{\text{products}} - \Sigma U_{\text{reactants}} \]

\(\Delta U < 0\) for exothermic processes; \(\Delta U > 0\) for endothermic processes.

Modes of Energy Transfer

  • Work (\(W\)): For gases, work is pressure-volume work:
    • Irreversible isothermal expansion: \(W = -p_{\text{ext}} \Delta V\).
    • Reversible isothermal expansion: \(W = -2.303 nRT \log\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right) = -2.303 nRT \log\left(\frac{p_1}{p_2}\right)\).
    • Reversible adiabatic expansion: \(W = \frac{nR (T_2 - T_1)}{\gamma - 1}\), where \(\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_V}\).
    • Irreversible adiabatic expansion: \(W = -p_{\text{ext}} \frac{nR (p_2 T_1 - p_1 T_2)}{p_1 p_2}\).
    • Free expansion (vacuum): \(W = 0\).
  • Heat (\(q\)): Transfers due to temperature difference, path-dependent, measured in joules or calories.

Heat Capacity

Heat capacity (\(C\)) is the heat required to raise a system’s temperature by 1°C:

  • Specific Heat Capacity: \(q = mc \Delta T\), where \(m\) is mass, \(c\) is specific heat.
  • Molar Heat Capacity: \(C_p = c_p M\), \(C_V = c_V M\), where \(M\) is molecular weight.
  • Relation: \(C_p - C_V = R\), where \(R\) is the gas constant.
  • For ideal gases: \(C_V = \frac{3}{2}R\), \(C_p = \frac{5}{2}R\), \(\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_V} = 1.66\) (monoatomic), 1.40 (diatomic), 1.33 (triatomic).

Calorimetry

  • Bomb Calorimeter (constant volume): \(\Delta U = -\text{heat absorbed by calorimeter}\).
  • Coffee-Cup Calorimeter (constant pressure): \(\Delta H = m c \Delta T_{\text{calorimeter}} + m c \Delta T_{\text{solution}}\).

7.3 Enthalpy and Thermochemistry

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define enthalpy and its relation to internal energy
  • Describe various forms of enthalpy changes
  • Apply laws of thermochemistry

Enthalpy (\(H\))

Enthalpy is the sum of internal energy and pressure-volume energy:

\[ H = U + pV \]

It is a state function and extensive property. The change in enthalpy is:

\[ \Delta H = H_2 - H_1 = \Sigma H_{\text{products}} - \Sigma H_{\text{reactants}} \]

\(\Delta H < 0\) for exothermic reactions; \(\Delta H > 0\) for endothermic reactions. Relation with internal energy:

\[ \Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta (pV) = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT \]

Where \(\Delta n_g\) is the change in moles of gas.

Enthalpy of Reaction (\(\Delta_r H\))

The enthalpy change for a balanced chemical reaction:

\[ \Delta_r H = \Sigma H_{\text{products}} - \Sigma H_{\text{reactants}} \]

Standard enthalpy (\(\Delta H^\circ\)) is at 1 atm and 298 K. Factors affecting \(\Delta_r H\): physical state, allotropic forms, chemical composition, amount of reactants, and temperature.

Forms of Enthalpy Change

  • Formation (\(\Delta_f H^\circ\)): Heat change when one mole of a compound forms from its elements; zero for elements in standard state.
  • Combustion (\(\Delta_C H^\circ\)): Heat change for complete combustion of one mole; always negative (exothermic).
  • Solution (\(\Delta_{\text{sol}} H^\circ\)): Heat change when one mole dissolves in excess solvent: \(\Delta_{\text{sol}} H^\circ = \Delta_{\text{lattice}} H^\circ + \Delta_{\text{hyd}} H^\circ\).
  • Hydration (\(\Delta_{\text{hyd}} H^\circ\)): Heat change when one mole of a salt combines with water; exothermic.
  • Fusion (\(\Delta_{\text{fus}} H^\circ\)): Heat change for melting one mole of a solid.
  • Vaporization (\(\Delta_{\text{vap}} H^\circ\)): Heat change for converting one mole of liquid to vapor.
  • Neutralization (\(\Delta_n H^\circ\)): Heat change when 1 g-equivalent of acid neutralizes 1 g-equivalent of base; 57.1 kJ for strong acid/base, less for weak acid/base.
  • Transition (\(\Delta_t H^\circ\)): Heat change for one mole transitioning between allotropic forms.
  • Atomization (\(\Delta_a H^\circ\)): Heat change to break one mole of a molecule into atoms.
  • Sublimation (\(\Delta_{\text{sub}} H^\circ\)): Heat change for one mole of solid subliming.
  • Lattice Enthalpy: Heat change when one mole of an ionic compound dissociates into gaseous ions.
  • Bond Enthalpy (\(\Delta_{\text{bond}} H^\circ\)): Energy to break one mole of bonds (bond dissociation enthalpy) or average for polyatomic molecules (mean bond enthalpy).

Laws of Thermochemistry

  • Lavoisier-Laplace Law: Enthalpy change of a reaction equals the negative of the reverse reaction’s enthalpy.
  • Hess’s Law: Total enthalpy change is the sum of enthalpies of intermediate steps: \(\Delta H = \Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 + \Delta H_3\).

7.4 Entropy and the Second Law

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define entropy and its changes
  • Explain the second law of thermodynamics
  • Calculate entropy changes for phase transitions

Entropy (\(S\))

Entropy measures molecular randomness or disorder, a state function and extensive property. Units: \(\text{J K}^{-1} \text{mol}^{-1}\). Entropy change:

\[ \Delta S = S_2 - S_1 = \frac{q_{\text{rev}}}{T} = \Sigma S_{\text{products}} - \Sigma S_{\text{reactants}} \]

\(\Delta S > 0\): increased randomness, heat absorbed; \(\Delta S < 0\): decreased randomness, heat evolved. For ideal gases:

\[ \Delta S = n C_V \ln\left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right) + n R \ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right) \]

Entropy in Phase Transitions

Entropy changes during phase transitions:

  • Fusion: \(\Delta S_{\text{melting}} = \frac{\Delta_{\text{fus}} H}{T_m}\), where \(T_m\) is the melting point.
  • Vaporization: \(\Delta S_{\text{vaporization}} = \frac{\Delta_{\text{vap}} H}{T_b}\), where \(T_b\) is the boiling point.
  • Sublimation: \(\Delta S_{\text{sublimation}} = \frac{\Delta_{\text{sub}} H}{T_{\text{sub}}}\), where \(T_{\text{sub}}\) is the sublimation temperature.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

The second law states that the entropy of the universe increases for spontaneous processes: \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} > 0\). At equilibrium, \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0\). All spontaneous processes are irreversible without external work.

Spontaneous Processes

A spontaneous process proceeds without external aid. Examples include sugar dissolving in water, water evaporation, and \(\ce{NO}\) reacting with \(\ce{O2}\). Some require initiation (e.g., burning coal, heating \(\ce{CaCO3}\)):

\[ \ce{CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g)} \]

Entropy Criterion

A process is spontaneous if \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0\). The enthalpy criterion (\(\Delta H < 0\)) fails for some endothermic spontaneous reactions, necessitating entropy or Gibbs energy criteria.

Gibbs Free Energy (\(G\))

Gibbs free energy is the energy available for useful work, a state function and extensive property:

\[ G = H - TS \]

Change in Gibbs energy:

\[ \Delta G = G_2 - G_1 = \Delta H - T \Delta S \]

Spontaneity criteria: \(\Delta G < 0\) (spontaneous), \(\Delta G > 0\) (non-spontaneous), \(\Delta G = 0\) (equilibrium).

Standard Free Energy (\(\Delta G^\circ\))

At standard conditions (1 atm, 298 K):

\[ \Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T \Delta S^\circ = \Sigma \Delta G_f^\circ (\text{products}) - \Sigma \Delta G_f^\circ (\text{reactants}) \]

For a reaction \(\ce{A + B <=> C + D}\), at equilibrium: \(\Delta G = 0\). Relation with equilibrium constant:

\[ \Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT \ln Q, \quad \Delta G^\circ = -RT \ln K = -2.303 RT \log K \]

Third Law of Thermodynamics

The third law states that the entropy of a perfectly crystalline substance at 0 K is zero. Absolute entropy:

\[ S = \int_0^T C_p \frac{dT}{T} = \int_0^T C_p \ln T \, dT \]

Imperfect crystals at 0 K have non-zero entropy.

Other Thermodynamic Concepts

  • Joule-Thomson Effect: Cooling of a gas during adiabatic expansion from high to low pressure. Coefficient: \(\mu = \frac{dT}{dp}\). Inversion temperature: \(T_i = \frac{2a}{Rb}\), where \(\mu = 0\).
  • Trouton’s Rule: \(\frac{\Delta_{\text{vap}} H}{T_b} \approx 88 \, \text{J mol}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}\).
  • Dulong-Petit Law: Specific heat \(\times\) molar mass = 6.4 cal mol\(^{-1}\) °C\(^{-1}\).
  • Kirchhoff’s Equation: \(\Delta H_2 = \Delta H_1 + \Delta C_p (T_2 - T_1)\), \(\Delta E_2 = \Delta E_1 + \Delta C_V (T_2 - T_1)\).
  • Clausius-Clapeyron Equation: \(\log \frac{p_2}{p_1} = -\frac{\Delta H_V}{2.303 R} \frac{T_2 - T_1}{T_1 T_2}\).
  • Carnot Cycle: Four-step cycle (isothermal expansion, adiabatic expansion, isothermal compression, adiabatic compression) with efficiency: \(\eta = \frac{T_2 - T_1}{T_2} = \frac{q_2 - q_1}{q_2} = \frac{w}{q_2}\).
  • Relation with EMF: \(\Delta G^\circ = -n F E_{\text{cell}}^\circ\), where \(n\) is the number of electrons, \(F = 96500 \, \text{C}\).

8.1 Introduction to Chemical Equilibrium

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define physical and chemical equilibrium
  • Describe characteristics of chemical equilibrium
  • Differentiate between homogeneous and heterogeneous equilibria

Chemical equilibrium is a state in a reversible reaction where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction, resulting in no net change in concentrations.

Physical and Chemical Processes

  • Physical Processes: Affect only physical properties without changing chemical composition (e.g., evaporation, melting).
  • Chemical Processes: Involve changes in chemical composition (e.g., \(\ce{PCl5(g) <=> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)}\)).

Equilibrium Types

  • Physical Equilibrium: Involves physical processes (e.g., \(\ce{H2O(s) <=> H2O(l)}\), rate of melting = rate of freezing).
  • Chemical Equilibrium: Involves reversible chemical reactions in a closed system, where \(R_f = R_b\).

Characteristics of Chemical Equilibrium

  • Attainable from either direction.
  • Dynamic, with continuous forward and backward reactions.
  • Constant concentrations of species.
  • Unaffected by catalysts, which only speed up attainment.
  • Requires a closed system.
  • Constant observable properties.

Homogeneous and Heterogeneous Equilibria

  • Homogeneous: All species in the same phase (e.g., \(\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}\)).
  • Heterogeneous: Species in different phases (e.g., \(\ce{3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) <=> Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g)}\)).

8.3 Law of Mass Action and Equilibrium Constant

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain the law of mass action
  • Calculate equilibrium constants (\(K_c\), \(K_p\))
  • Understand reaction quotient and its relation to equilibrium

Law of Mass Action

The law of mass action (Guldberg and Waage) states that the rate of a chemical reaction is proportional to the product of the active masses of reactants. For a reaction:

\[ aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD \]

Forward rate: \(R_f = k_f [A]^a [B]^b\), backward rate: \(R_b = k_b [C]^c [D]^d\). At equilibrium, \(R_f = R_b\):

\[ K_c = \frac{k_f}{k_b} = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b} \]

In heterogeneous equilibria, active masses of pure solids and liquids are taken as 1.

Equilibrium Constant

\(K_c\) uses molar concentrations; \(K_p\) uses partial pressures for gases:

\[ K_p = \frac{p_C^c \cdot p_D^d}{p_A^a \cdot p_B^b} \]

Relation: \(K_p = K_c (RT)^{\Delta n}\), where \(\Delta n = (c + d) - (a + b)\). Units: \(K_p = (\text{atm})^{\Delta n}\), \(K_c = (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{\Delta n}\).

Characteristics of Equilibrium Constant

  • Definite value at a given temperature.
  • Larger \(K_c\) or \(K_p\) indicates greater reaction completion (\(K \geq 10\): near completion; \(K \leq 10^{-3}\): negligible reaction).
  • For composite reactions, \(K_c = K_{c1} \cdot K_{c2}\).
  • Independent of initial concentrations and catalysts.
  • Inverse for backward reaction: \(K_{c,\text{backward}} = 1/K_{c,\text{forward}}\).
  • If reaction is multiplied by \(n\), \(K\) becomes \(K^n\); if divided by \(m\), \(K\) becomes \(K^{1/m}\).

Reaction Quotient (\(Q_c\))

For a reaction not at equilibrium:

\[ Q_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b} \]

Comparison with \(K_c\): \(Q_c > K_c\) (backward reaction favored), \(Q_c < K_c\) (forward reaction favored), \(Q_c = K_c\) (equilibrium).

Gibbs Free Energy and Equilibrium

Relation with Gibbs free energy:

\[ \Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + 2.303 RT \log Q_c \]

At equilibrium (\(\Delta G = 0\), \(Q_c = K_c\)):

\[ \Delta G^\circ = -2.303 RT \log K_c \quad \text{or} \quad \Delta G^\circ = -2.303 RT \log K_p \]

Ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium.
reaction quotient
Ratio of concentrations at any point in a reaction.

8.4 Le-Chatelier’s Principle

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain Le-Chatelier’s principle
  • Predict effects of concentration, pressure, and temperature changes
  • Apply the principle to physical and chemical equilibria

Le-Chatelier’s Principle

Le-Chatelier’s principle states that if a system at equilibrium is disturbed by changes in concentration, pressure, or temperature, the equilibrium shifts to counteract the change.

Effects on Chemical Equilibria

  • Concentration: Increasing reactant concentration shifts equilibrium forward; increasing product concentration shifts it backward.
  • Pressure: For reactions with \(\Delta n \neq 0\), increased pressure favors the side with fewer gas moles (e.g., \(\ce{N2 + 3H2 <=> 2NH3}\), high pressure favors \(\ce{NH3}\)). No effect if \(\Delta n = 0\) (e.g., \(\ce{N2 + O2 <=> 2NO}\)).
  • Temperature: Exothermic reactions (\(\Delta H < 0\)) favor products at low temperatures; endothermic reactions (\(\Delta H > 0\)) favor products at high temperatures.
  • Inert Gas: At constant pressure, adding inert gas increases volume, favoring more gas moles. At constant volume, no effect.
  • Catalyst: Speeds up equilibrium attainment but does not shift equilibrium.

Le-Chatelier’s Principle in Physical Equilibria

  • Solubility: Increased pressure increases gas solubility; for solids, exothermic dissolution decreases with temperature (e.g., \(\ce{Ca(OH)2}\)), endothermic increases (e.g., \(\ce{KNO3}\)).
  • Melting Point: For \(\ce{H2O(s) <=> H2O(l)}\), increased pressure favors liquid (less volume).

\(\ce{PCl5 <=> PCl3 + Cl2}\) Low pressure, high temperature, isolate \(\ce{PCl3}\) and \(\ce{Cl2}\)

9.1 Electrolytes and Degree of Dissociation

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

Electrolytes and Non-Electrolytes

  • Non-Electrolytes: Do not ionize in solution, poor conductors (e.g., urea, glucose).
  • Electrolytes: Conduct electricity via ion movement in aqueous or molten state.
  • Strong Electrolytes: Fully dissociate (e.g., \(\ce{HCl}\), \(\ce{NaOH}\), except \(\ce{HgCl2}\), \(\ce{CdBr2}\)).
  • Weak Electrolytes: Partially dissociate (e.g., \(\ce{CH3COOH}\), \(\ce{NH4OH}\), \(\ce{HCN}\)).

Degree of Dissociation (\(\alpha\))

The fraction of molecules that dissociate into ions:

\[ \alpha = \frac{\text{number of molecules dissociated}}{\text{total number of molecules}} \]

For strong electrolytes, \(\alpha = 1\); for weak electrolytes, \(\alpha < 1\). Factors affecting \(\alpha\): nature of solute/solvent, concentration, temperature, other species.

Calculation of \(\alpha\)

Using vapor density:

\[ \alpha = \frac{D - d}{d (y - 1)} \]

Where \(D\) is theoretical vapor density, \(d\) is observed vapor density, \(y\) is moles of products per mole of reactant. Alternatively:

\[ \alpha = \frac{M_c - M_o}{M_o} \]

Where \(M_c\) is calculated molecular weight, \(M_o\) is observed molecular weight.

Ostwald’s Dilution Law

For a weak electrolyte:

\[ K = \frac{\alpha^2 C}{1 - \alpha} \]

If \(\alpha\) is small, \(1 - \alpha \approx 1\), then:

\[ \alpha = \sqrt{\frac{K}{C}}, \quad \alpha \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{C}} \]

Where \(K\) is the dissociation constant, \(C\) is molar concentration.

9.2 Acids and Bases

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Describe Arrhenius, Bronsted-Lowry, and Lewis concepts

Acid-Base Concepts

  • Arrhenius: Acids produce \(\ce{H+}\)/\(\ce{H3O+}\) in water; bases produce \(\ce{OH-}\). Fails in non-aqueous solutions (e.g., \(\ce{AlCl3}\), \(\ce{NH3}\)).
  • Bronsted-Lowry: Acids donate protons; bases accept protons. Example:
    • \[ \ce{NH3 + H2O <=> NH4+ + OH-} \]

    Conjugate pairs: \(\ce{NH3}/\ce{NH4+}\), \(\ce{H2O}/\ce{OH-}\). Strong acids have weak conjugate bases; weak acids have strong conjugate bases.

  • Lewis: Acids accept electron pairs (e.g., \(\ce{BF3}\), \(\ce{Ag+}\)); bases donate electron pairs (e.g., \(\ce{NH3}\), \(\ce{OH-}\)).

Relative Strengths

Stronger acids have larger \(K_a\) (smaller \(pK_a\)); stronger bases have larger \(K_b\) (smaller \(pK_b\)). Relative strength:

\[ \frac{\alpha_1}{\alpha_2} = \sqrt{\frac{K_{a1}}{K_{a2}}} = \frac{\text{strength of } \ce{HA1}}{\text{strength of } \ce{HA2}} \]

Acidic strength: \(\ce{HClO4 > HI > HBr > H2SO4 > HCl > HNO3}\). \(\ce{CsOH}\) is the strongest base.

Leveling Effect

Strong acids in water level to the strength of \(\ce{H3O+}\); strong bases level to \(\ce{OH-}\).

Amphoteric Substances

Act as both acid and base (e.g., \(\ce{H2O}\), acts as acid with \(\ce{NH3}\), base with \(\ce{CH3COOH}\)).

9.4 Buffer Solutions and Solubility Product

  • Explain buffer solutions and their pH calculation

Buffer Solutions

Buffer solutions resist pH changes upon addition of small amounts of acid or base.

  • Acidic Buffer: pH < 7 (e.g., \(\ce{CH3COOH/CH3COONa}\)).
  • Basic Buffer: pH > 7 (e.g., \(\ce{NH4OH/NH4Cl}\)).
  • Blood buffer: \(\ce{H2CO3/NaHCO3}\).

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

For acidic buffers:

\[ \text{pH} = \text{p}K_a + \log \frac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]} \]

For basic buffers:

\[ \text{pOH} = \text{p}K_b + \log \frac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{base}]}, \quad \text{pH} = 14 - \text{pOH} \]

Buffer capacity (\(\phi\)): moles of acid/base added per liter to change pH by 1. Buffer fails if \(\text{pH} = \text{p}K_a \pm 1\).

Solubility Product (\(K_{\text{sp}}\))

For a salt \(\ce{A_xB_y <=> xA^{y+} + yB^{x-}}\):

\[ K_{\text{sp}} = [\ce{A^{y+}}]^x [\ce{B^{x-}}]^y \]

Applications: Predicts precipitation (\(\text{ionic product} > K_{\text{sp}}\): precipitate forms). Solubility (\(s\)) for:

  • \(\ce{AB}\): \(K_{\text{sp}} = s^2\), \(s = \sqrt{K_{\text{sp}}}\).
  • \(\ce{AB2}\): \(K_{\text{sp}} = 4s^3\), \(s = \left(\frac{K_{\text{sp}}}{4}\right)^{1/3}\).
  • \(\ce{AB3}\): \(K_{\text{sp}} = 27s^4\), \(s = \left(\frac{K_{\text{sp}}}{27}\right)^{1/4}\).

Common Ion Effect

Suppression of weak electrolyte dissociation by adding a strong electrolyte with a common ion (e.g., \(\ce{NH4OH}\) dissociation decreases with \(\ce{NH4Cl}\)). Used in:

  • Purification of common salt.
  • Salting out of soap.
  • Qualitative analysis (e.g., group II precipitation with \(\ce{HCl}\)).

Concentration Definitions

Concentration is the relative amount of solute in a solution. Solutions are dilute (small solute quantity) or concentrated (large solute quantity).

Methods of Expressing Concentration

  • Percentage by Weight (w/w %): Weight of solute per 100 g of solution.
    • \[ w/w\% = \frac{\text{weight of solute}}{\text{weight of solution}} \times 100 \]
  • Percentage by Volume (v/V %): Volume of solute per 100 mL of solution.
    • \[ v/V\% = \frac{\text{volume of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times 100 \]
  • Percentage Mass by Volume (w/V %): Weight of solute per 100 mL of solution.
    • \[ w/V\% = \frac{\text{weight of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times 100 \]
  • Mole Fraction (\(\chi\)): Ratio of moles of a component to total moles.
    • \[ \chi_A = \frac{n_A}{n_A + n_B}, \quad \chi_A + \chi_B = 1 \]
  • Parts per Million (ppm): Parts of component per million parts of solution.
    • \[ \text{ppm} = \frac{\text{number of parts of component}}{\text{total parts}} \times 10^6 \]
  • Molarity (M): Moles of solute per liter of solution.
    • \[ M = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}} \]

    Dilution Law: \( M_1 V_1 = M_2 V_2 \). For mixing solutions:

    \[ M = \frac{M_1 V_1 + M_2 V_2}{V_1 + V_2} \]
  • Molality (m): Moles of solute per kg of solvent.
    • \[ m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}} \]
  • Normality (N): Gram equivalents of solute per liter of solution.
    • \[ N = \frac{\text{gram equivalents of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}} \]

    Relation: \( N \times \text{eq. wt.} = M \times \text{mol. wt.} \).

  • Formality (F): Formula weights of solute per liter of solution.
  • Mass Fraction: Mass of component divided by total mass.
    • \[ \text{Mass fraction} = \frac{\text{mass of component}}{\text{total mass}} \]
  • Demal (D): One mole of solute per liter at 0°C.

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

Vapour Pressure

Vapour pressure is the pressure exerted by vapour molecules in equilibrium with a liquid at a given temperature. Factors affecting it include:

  • Purity: Pure liquids have higher vapour pressure than solutions.
  • Nature of Liquid: Weaker intermolecular forces increase vapour pressure.
  • Temperature: Increases with temperature.
  • Solute Addition: Non-volatile solutes decrease vapour pressure.

Raoult’s Law

For a solution of volatile liquids, the partial vapour pressure of each component is proportional to its mole fraction:

\[ p_A = p_A^\circ \chi_A, \quad p_B = p_B^\circ \chi_B \]

Total vapour pressure:

\[ p = p_A^\circ \chi_A + p_B^\circ \chi_B \]

Raoult’s law is a special case of Henry’s law when \(K_H = p^\circ\).

Ideal Solutions

Ideal solutions obey Raoult’s law, with similar solute-solvent and solvent-solvent interactions:

\[ p_A = p_A^\circ \chi_A, \quad \Delta H = 0, \quad \Delta V = 0 \]

Examples: benzene + toluene, n-hexane + n-heptane.

Non-Ideal Solutions

Non-ideal solutions deviate from Raoult’s law:

  • Positive Deviation: Weaker A-B interactions; \( p > p_A^\circ \chi_A + p_B^\circ \chi_B \), \(\Delta H > 0\), \(\Delta V > 0\). Examples: ethanol + water.
  • Negative Deviation: Stronger A-B interactions; \( p < p_A^\circ \chi_A + p_B^\circ \chi_B \), \(\Delta H < 0\), \(\Delta V < 0\). Examples: chloroform + acetone.

Azeotropic Mixtures

Azeotropes boil at a constant temperature with unchanged composition:

  • Minimum Boiling Azeotropes: Positive deviation, lower boiling point (e.g., 95.57% ethanol + 4.43% water).
  • Maximum Boiling Azeotropes: Negative deviation, higher boiling point (e.g., 20.22% water + 79.78% HCl).

Colligative properties depend on the number of solute particles, not their nature.

\[ \frac{p^\circ - p}{p^\circ} = \chi_B \approx \frac{n_B}{n_A} \]

11.5 Balancing Redox Reactions

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Balance redox reactions using ion-electron method
  • Balance redox reactions using oxidation number method
  • Apply balancing to real-life redox processes

Ion-Electron Method

Steps for acidic medium (e.g., \(\ce{Cu + HNO3 -> Cu(NO3)2 + NO + H2O}\)):

  1. Write ionic form: \(\ce{Cu + H+ + NO3- -> Cu^2+ + NO + H2O}\).
  2. Split into half-reactions: \(\ce{Cu -> Cu^2+}\), \(\ce{NO3- -> NO}\).
  3. Balance atoms (except H, O).
  4. Balance O with \(\ce{H2O}\), H with \(\ce{H+}\).
  5. Add electrons: \(\ce{Cu -> Cu^2+ + 2e-}\), \(\ce{3e- + 4H+ + NO3- -> NO + 2H2O}\).
  6. Equalize electrons and combine.
  7. Add spectator ions: \(\ce{3Cu + 8HNO3 -> 3Cu(NO3)2 + 2NO + 4H2O}\).

Oxidation Number Method

Steps (e.g., \(\ce{Mg + HNO3 -> Mg(NO3)2 + N2O + H2O}\)):

  1. Assign oxidation numbers.
  2. Identify changes: Mg (0 to +2), N (+5 to +1).
  3. Balance atoms and equalize oxidation number changes.
  4. Balance H and O: \(\ce{4Mg + 10HNO3 -> 4Mg(NO3)2 + N2O + 5H2O}\).

Redox in Daily Life

Examples: corrosion, bleaching, photosynthesis, metallurgy.

13.10 Arrhenius Equation

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

The s- and p-Block Elements

s-Block Elements

In the s-block elements, the last electron enters the s-orbital. As the s-orbital can accommodate only two electrons, two groups (1 and 2) belong to the s-block. The general electronic configuration of s-block elements is ns¹⁻².

Alkali Metals [Group-I]

Group-I elements have one electron in their valence shell. They do not occur in the native or free state. These elements are collectively known as alkali metals because their oxides and hydroxides form strong alkalies like NaOH, KOH, etc. Lithium is known as a bridge element.

General Characteristics

  • Electronic configuration: [noble gas] ns¹
  • Atomic radii: Largest in their respective periods, increases down the group.
  • Ionic radii: Smaller than corresponding metals, increases down the group.
  • Density: Light metals, increases from Li to Cs, with potassium less dense than sodium.
  • Melting and boiling points: Low, decrease down the group.
  • Softness: Soft, malleable, ductile, can be cut with a knife.
  • Ionisation enthalpy: Lowest in their periods, decreases down the group.
  • Electropositive character: Strongly electropositive, increases from Li to Cs.
  • Oxidation state: Show +1 oxidation state.
  • Flame colouration: Impart characteristic colours to flames (e.g., Li: Crimson, Na: Yellow).
  • Photoelectric effect: Cs exhibits due to low ionisation enthalpy.
  • Electrical conductivity: Good conductors, increases from Li to Cs.
  • Reducing character: Strong reducing agents, order: Na < K < Rb < Cs < Li.

Chemical Properties

  • Action of air: Form oxides, hydroxides, carbonates; stored in kerosene or paraffin wax.
  • Action of oxygen: Form oxides, peroxides, superoxides; stability increases down the group.
  • Action of water: React to form hydroxides and hydrogen, reactivity: Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs.
  • Action of hydrogen: Form ionic hydrides, reactivity: Li > Na > K > Rb > Cs.
  • Reaction with halogens: Form ionic halides, reactivity increases down the group.
  • Solubility in liquid ammonia: Form deep blue solutions due to ammoniated electrons.
  • Nature of carbonates and bicarbonates: Thermal stability increases down the group.
  • Nature of nitrates: LiNO₃ decomposes to NO₂ and O₂, others to nitrites and O₂.
  • Nature of sulphates: Li₂SO₄ insoluble, others soluble.

Uses of Alkali Metals

Lithium in alloys, batteries; sodium in alloys, coolant; potassium in fertilizers.

Anomalous Behaviour of Lithium

Due to small size, high ionisation enthalpy, and no d-orbitals, lithium differs in hardness, oxide formation, and solubility.

Diagonal Relationship

Lithium resembles magnesium due to similar polarising power.

Compounds of Sodium

  • Sodium Chloride (NaCl): Used as table salt, in chemical preparations.
  • Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH): Used in soap, paper, dyes, and petrol refining.
  • Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O): Used in water softening, textiles.
  • Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃): Used in baking, as an antacid.
  • Microcosmic Salt (Na(NH₄)HPO₄·4H₂O): Used in bead tests for metal detection.

Alkaline Earth Metals [Group-II]

Include Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra with two valence electrons. Called alkaline earth metals due to alkaline oxides found in earth’s crust.

General Characteristics

  • Electronic configuration: [noble gas] ns²
  • Atomic and ionic radii: Large but smaller than alkali metals, increase down the group.
  • Density: Denser than alkali metals, decreases from Be to Ca, then increases.
  • Melting and boiling points: Higher than alkali metals, decrease down the group.
  • Metallic properties: Silvery white, harder than alkali metals.
  • Ionisation enthalpy: Higher than alkali metals, decreases down the group.
  • Electropositive character: Strong, increases down the group.
  • Oxidation state: Uniformly +2.
  • Flame colouration: Ca, Sr, Ba impart characteristic colours; Be, Mg do not.
  • Crystal lattice: Be, Mg (hcp); Ca, Sr (ccp); Ba (bcc).

Chemical Properties

  • Reaction with water: Less reactive than alkali metals, reactivity: Mg < Ca < Sr < Ba.
  • Reaction with oxygen: Form monoxides or peroxides.
  • Reaction with acids: Displace H₂, reactivity increases down the group.
  • Reaction with hydrogen: Form hydrides, except Be.
  • Reaction with nitrogen: Form nitrides, hydrolysed to NH₃.
  • Reaction with carbon: Form carbides, except Be.
  • Reducing character: Strong, increases from Be to Ra.
  • Solubility in liquid ammonia: Form coloured solutions.
  • Complex formation: Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺ form complexes with EDTA.
  • Basic strength: BeO, Be(OH)₂ amphoteric; others basic.

20.1 Transition Elements

Learning Objectives

Transition elements are those with partially filled d-orbitals in their ground or excited states, located in groups 3–12 of the periodic table. Iron is the most abundant and widely used transition metal.

20.2 General Properties of Transition Elements

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the concept of lanthanoid contraction and its effects.

Transition elements exhibit unique properties due to their partially filled d-orbitals:

  • Atomic and Ionic Size: Ionic radii decrease with increasing atomic number due to increased nuclear charge. Lanthanoid contraction causes 5d-series elements to have similar sizes to 4d-series counterparts.
  • Ionization Enthalpies: Increase irregularly across a series due to varying orbital energies.

  • Catalytic Properties: Facilitate reactions via variable oxidation states and surface adsorption (e.g., Fe in Haber’s process).
  • Magnetic Properties: Paramagnetism arises from unpaired d-electrons, with magnetic moment given by \(\mu = \sqrt{n(n+2)}\) BM.
  • Coloured Ions: Due to d-d transitions (e.g., \(\ce{[Cu(H2O)4]^2+}\) is blue).

Coordination compounds are molecular compounds with a central metal atom or ion bonded to ligands via coordinate bonds. 21.1 Introduction to Coordination Compounds

Learning Objectives

  • Define coordination compounds and distinguish them from double salts.
  • Understand the components of a coordination entity.

Coordination compounds, such as \(\ce{K4[Fe(CN)6]}\), consist of a central metal atom/ion surrounded by ligands, forming a coordination sphere. Unlike double salts (e.g., Mohr’s salt, \(\ce{FeSO4 \cdot (NH4)2SO4 \cdot 6H2O}\)), which dissociate into ions in solution, coordination compounds remain intact. The central metal, typically a transition or inner-transition element, acts as a Lewis acid, accepting electron pairs from ligands.

Coordination Compound
A compound with a central metal bonded to ligands via coordinate bonds.
Double Salt
A compound that dissociates into ions in solution.
Coordination Entity
The central metal and its ligands, enclosed in a coordination sphere.

21.2 Ligands and Coordination Number

Learning Objectives

  • Identify types of ligands and their denticity.
  • Calculate the coordination number of a complex.
  • Understand IUPAC nomenclature for coordination compounds.

Ligands are electron-donating species bonded to the central metal. They are classified as:

  • Unidentate: One donor site (e.g., \(\ce{NH3}\), \(\ce{H2O}\)).
  • Didentate: Two donor sites (e.g., oxalate, ethylenediamine).
  • Polydentate: Multiple donor sites (e.g., EDTA, hexadentate).
  • Ambidentate: Can bind via different sites (e.g., \(\ce{NO2^-}\)).
  • Chelating: Form rings with the metal (e.g., en).
The coordination number (CN) is the number of coordinate bonds, calculated as the number of ligands times their denticity. IUPAC naming follows rules like naming the cation first, using prefixes (di-, tri-, bis-), and indicating oxidation states (e.g., \(\ce{[Cr(NH3)3(H2O)3]Cl3}\): triamminetriaquachromium(III) chloride).

Key Terms

Ligand
An ion or molecule that donates electrons to a central metal.
Coordination Number
Number of coordinate bonds formed by the central metal.
Chelate Effect
Increased stability due to chelating ligands forming rings.

21.4 Bonding Theories in Coordination Compounds

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Werner’s theory and its limitations.
  • Understand valence bond theory (VBT) and crystal field theory (CFT).
  • Describe the color and stability of coordination compounds.

Bonding in coordination compounds is explained by:

  • Werner’s Theory: Proposes primary (ionizable) and secondary (non-ionizable, directional) valencies, determining geometry. Limitations include inability to explain magnetic properties.
  • Valence Bond Theory (VBT): Metal provides empty orbitals (e.g., \(\ce{d^2sp^3}\) for octahedral \(\ce{[Co(NH3)6]^3+}\)) for ligand electrons. Inner (low-spin) or outer (high-spin) orbitals determine magnetic properties.
  • Crystal Field Theory (CFT): Ligands as point charges split d-orbitals into \(\ce{t2g}\) and \(\ce{eg}\) sets, with splitting energy \(\Delta_o\). Strong-field ligands form low-spin complexes; weak-field ligands form high-spin complexes. Color arises from d-d transitions (e.g., \(\ce{[Ti(H2O)6]^3+}\) is violet).

Key Terms

Valence Bond Theory
Explains bonding via hybridization of metal orbitals.
Crystal Field Theory
Describes d-orbital splitting due to ligand interactions.
Spectrochemical Series
Order of ligands by increasing crystal field splitting.

Review Questions

  1. Which theory explains the color of coordination compounds?
    1. Werner’s theory
    2. Valence bond theory
    3. Crystal field theory
    4. Molecular orbital theory

Chapter 23: Purification and Characterisation of Organic Compounds

Purification of Organic Compounds

Organic compounds extracted from a natural source or synthesized in the laboratory require purification. Various methods are used for purification, based on the nature of the compound and the impurities present. The purity of a compound is ascertained by determining its melting point, boiling point, or by chromatographic and spectroscopic techniques.

Methods of Purification of Solids

  1. Crystallisation: A saturated solution of the impure substance is prepared in a hot solvent and heated with animal charcoal to adsorb impurities. The solution is filtered, and the filtrate deposits crystals of the pure compound upon cooling. The success depends on selecting a solvent in which impurities are least soluble. Seeding initiates crystal formation by adding crystals of the pure substance.
  2. Fractional Crystallisation: Based on different solubilities in a solvent, the less soluble compound crystallizes first on cooling, leaving others in solution. Mixtures of solvents (e.g., alcohol and water, chloroform and petroleum ether) may yield better results.
  3. Sublimation: Some solids convert directly to vapors upon heating without becoming liquid (sublimates). This process, called sublimation, purifies substances like camphor, naphthalene, and anthracene, provided impurities do not sublime.

Methods of Purification of Liquids

  1. Simple Distillation: Involves vaporization by heating and condensation by cooling. Suitable for liquids boiling under normal conditions without decomposition and containing non-volatile impurities.
  2. Fractional Distillation: Separates mixtures of volatile liquids with close boiling points (e.g., acetone at 60°C and methanol at 65°C). Vapors pass through a fractionating column, allowing high boiling point substances to condense and return to the flask. Used in petroleum component separation.
  3. Distillation Under Reduced Pressure or Vacuum Distillation: Purifies liquids that decompose at their boiling points (e.g., glycerol) by distilling at lower temperatures under reduced pressure.
  4. Steam Distillation: Purifies water-insoluble, steam-volatile liquids with high molecular weight and vapor pressure. The liquid boils when the sum of vapor pressures of the organic liquid (p1) and water (p2) equals atmospheric pressure (p = p1 + p2). Since p1 is lower than p, the liquid vaporizes below its boiling point. Example: o-hydroxy acetophenone and p-hydroxy acetophenone are separated this way.
  5. By Separating Funnel: Separates immiscible liquids via differential extraction using a separating funnel.

Chromatographic Method

Discovered by Tswett in 1906, chromatography relies on selective adsorption between a stationary phase (solid or ion exchange resin) and a mobile phase (liquid or gas). Techniques include:

  1. Adsorption Chromatography:
    • Column Chromatography: Uses adsorbents like alumina, silica gel, or cellulose powder. Solvents like benzene or petroleum ether are poured over a column, separating components into zones due to differential adsorption.
    • Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC): Involves a thin layer (0.2 mm) of adsorbent (silica gel or alumina) on a glass plate. A mixture is applied as a spot, and the plate is placed in a solvent (eluant). Components move different distances based on adsorption, enabling separation. The retardation factor (Rf) is calculated as:
      Rf = (distance moved by substance from baseline (x)) / (distance moved by solvent from baseline (y))
  2. Partition Chromatography: Uses a liquid stationary phase on an inert solid, with a liquid or gas mobile phase. Known as liquid-liquid or liquid-gas partition chromatography.
  3. Paper Chromatography: Solutes migrate at different rates on filter paper via capillary action. A sample is applied 2-3 cm from the paper’s edge, dried, and suspended in a solvent-saturated container. The Rf value is:
    Rf = (distance from starting line to zone center) / (distance from starting line to solvent front)

Elution: Continuous solvent pouring from the column top, with weakly adsorbed components eluting first with less polar solvents, followed by strongly adsorbed ones with polar solvents.

Chemical Methods of Purification

The substance is treated with a chemical reagent to form a stable derivative, separated, and decomposed to yield the pure compound. Examples:

  1. Separation of amines (1°, 2°, 3°) by Hinsberg’s method.
  2. Acetic acid from pyroligneous acid via calcium salt formation.
  3. Acids separated by forming sodium derivatives with NaHCO3.
  4. Absolute alcohol from rectified spirit using quick lime and azeotropic distillation.

Azeotropic Distillation

Azeotropes are constant boiling mixtures that distill without composition change. Example: rectified spirit (95.87% ethanol, 4.23% water) boils at 351.1 K. Separation involves adding a component (e.g., C6H6) to form a new, lower-boiling heterogeneous azeotrope.

Qualitative Analysis of Organic Compounds

Detection of Carbon and Hydrogen

Heating with dry CuO oxidizes carbon to CO2 and hydrogen to H2O:

C + 2CuO → CO2 + 2Cu

2H + CuO → H2O + Cu

CO2 turns lime water milky:

Ca(OH)2 + CO2 → CaCO3↓ + H2O

H2O turns anhydrous CuSO4 blue:

CuSO4 + 5H2O → CuSO4·5H2O

Lassaigne’s Test

Fusion with Na converts elements (N, S, X) to NaCN, Na2S, or NaX. The sodium extract is tested:

  1. Nitrogen: Add freshly prepared FeSO4, warm, cool, and acidify with H2SO4. Green or Prussian blue indicates nitrogen:

    6CN− + Fe2+ → [Fe(CN)6]4−

    3[Fe(CN)6]4− + 4Fe3+ → Fe4[Fe(CN)6]3 · xH2O (Prussian blue)

    If S is present, a red color (Fe(SCN)2+) appears instead. Hydrazine requires added charcoal/starch for a positive test. Diazonium salts do not show this test due to N2 loss on heating.

  2. Sulphur:
    • With acetic acid and lead acetate, a black PbS precipitate indicates sulphur:

      • S2− + Pb2+ → PbS↓ (black)

    • With sodium nitroprusside, a violet color confirms sulphur:

      • S2− + [Fe(CN)5NO]2− → [Fe(CN)5NOS]4− (violet)

  3. Halogens: Acidify with HNO3, then add AgNO3. Precipitates indicate halogens:

    4. X− + Ag+ → AgX↓ (X = Cl: white; Br: dull yellow; I: bright yellow)

    Note: Beilstein test is not confirmatory for halogens.

  4. Phosphorus: Oxidize with Na2O2 to phosphate, boil with HNO3, and add ammonium molybdate. A yellow precipitate indicates phosphorus:

    5. Na3PO4 + 3HNO3 → H3PO4 + 3NaNO3

    H3PO4 + 12(NH4)2MoO4 + 21HNO3 → (NH4)3PO4·12MoO3↓ + 21NH4NO3 + 12H2O

Quantitative Estimation of Elements

Estimation of Carbon and Hydrogen (Liebig’s Method)

Heat with CuO to oxidize C to CO2 and H to H2O:

CxHyOz + (x + y/4)CuO → xCO2 + (y/2)H2O + (x + y/4)Cu

Absorb H2O in CaCl2 and CO2 in KOH. Calculate:

%C = (12/44) × (mass of CO2 / mass of compound) × 100

%H = (2/18) × (mass of H2O / mass of compound) × 100

Remove nitrogen oxides with Cu gauge, halogens with Ag gauge, and sulphur with lead chromate.

Estimation of Nitrogen

  1. Duma’s Method: Heat with CuO in CO2, pass over Cu gauze to reduce nitrogen oxides to N2, collect over KOH:

    2. CxHyNz + (x + y/4)CuO → xCO2 + (y/2)H2O + (z/2)N2 + (x + y/4)Cu

    %N = (28/22400) × (volume of N2 at NTP / mass of compound) × 100

  2. Kjeldahl’s Method: Heat with conc. H2SO4, K2SO4, and CuSO4 to form (NH4)2SO4, then with NaOH to liberate NH3, absorbed in standard H2SO4:

    3. Organic compound + H2SO4 → (NH4)2SO4 → NH3 + Na2SO4

    2NH3 + H2SO4 → (NH4)2SO4

    %N = 1.4 × N × V / mass of compound (N = normality of acid, V = volume of acid neutralized)

    Not reliable for nitro, nitroso, azo, or ring nitrogen compounds.

Estimation of Halogen (Carius Method)

Heat with fuming HNO3 and AgNO3 to form AgX:

Organic compound + HNO3 + AgNO3 → AgX↓

%Halogen = (atomic mass of halogen × mass of AgX / molecular mass of AgX × mass of compound) × 100

Estimation of Sulphur

Heat with Na2O2 or HNO3 to form H2SO4, precipitate as BaSO4:

Organic compound + Na2O2/HNO3 → H2SO4 → BaSO4↓

%S = (32/233) × (mass of BaSO4 / mass of compound) × 100

Estimation of Phosphorus

Heat with HNO3 to form H3PO4, precipitate as (NH4)3PO4·12MoO3 or MgNH4PO4, then ignite to Mg2P2O7:

Organic compound + HNO3 → H3PO4 → MgNH4PO4 → Mg2P2O7

%P = (62/222) × (mass of Mg2P2O7 / mass of compound) × 100

Estimation of Oxygen

Convert oxygen to CO, then oxidize with I2O5 to CO2 and I2:

2CO + I2O5 → 5CO2 + I2

%O = (32/88) × (mass of CO2 / mass of compound) × 100

Chapter 24: General Organic Chemistry

Organic Chemistry

Organic compounds are hydrides of carbon (hydrocarbons) and their derivatives. Organic chemistry studies these compounds. Berzelius (1808) defined it as the chemistry of substances in living matter, proposing the vital force theory. Wöhler’s synthesis of urea disproved this:

(NH4)2SO4 + 2KCNO → K2SO4 + 2NH4CNO → NH2CONH2 (urea)

Acetic acid was the first organic compound synthesized from its elements.

Reasons for Large Number of Organic Compounds

  1. Catenation: Carbon’s ability to form single, double, or triple bonds with other carbon atoms.
  2. Tetravalency and Small Size: Carbon bonds with four atoms, including C, O, H, Cl, S, N, and P, forming stable compounds due to its small size.

General Characteristics of Organic Compounds

  1. Contain C with H, O, N, S, P, F, Cl, Br, I.
  2. Found in living organisms (e.g., carbohydrates, proteins).
  3. May be gases, liquids, or solids.
  4. Covalent, with low boiling/melting points, soluble in organic solvents.
  5. Volatile, inflammable, non-conductive, with distinct color/odor.

Representation of Different Formula

  1. Complete Formula: Shows all bonds, e.g., CH3CHClCH2CH3.
  2. Condensed Formula: Omits some bonds, e.g., CH3CH(Cl)CH2CH3.
  3. Bond Line Formula: Lines represent bonds, terminals/folds represent carbons, assuming H atoms for tetravalency, e.g., for CH3CH(CH3)CH2CH3.

Three-dimensional Representation

Uses solid and dashed wedges to depict 3D structure, e.g., CH4 with bonds in/out of the plane.

Classification of Organic Compounds

  • Open Chain (Aliphatic): Alkanes, alkenes.
  • Closed Chain (Cyclic):
    • Homocyclic/Carbocyclic: Alicyclic (e.g., cyclopropane), aromatic (benzenoid: benzene; non-benzenoid: tropolone).
    • Heterocyclic: Furan, pyrrole, thiophene.

Classification of Carbon Atoms

  1. By Number of C Attached: Primary (1°C), secondary (2°C), tertiary (3°C), quaternary (4°C). Reactivity: 3° > 2° > 1°.
  2. By Position: α-carbon (attached to functional group), β-carbon (attached to α-carbon).

Hydrogen Classification: 1°-, 2°-, 3°-, α-, β-hydrogens based on attached carbon.

Functional Group

Atoms (e.g., Cl, Br) or groups (e.g., COOH, CHO) responsible for chemical properties. Double/triple bonds are also functional groups. Alkyl (single bond), alkenyl (double bond), alkynyl (triple bond).

Homologous Series

Series differing by CH2 units, e.g., alkenes (C2H4, C3H6). Characteristics:

  1. Same functional group, similar chemical properties.
  2. Same general formula (e.g., alkanes: CnH2n+2).
  3. Similar preparation methods.
  4. Gradual variation in physical properties.

Nomenclature of Organic Compounds

Trivial System

Names from source/property, e.g., acetic acid (from acetum = vinegar).

IUPAC System

Rules (1993 recommendations):

  1. Longest Chain Rule: Select the longest chain with the principal functional group, secondary group, or multiple bonds.
  2. Lowest Number Rule: Number to give lowest numbers to branches, side chains, or functional groups.
  3. Prefixes and Suffixes: Prefix for substituents, suffix for functional groups (e.g., -ol for alcohols).

Examples: 4-hydroxybutanal, propane-1,2,3-tricarbonitrile, cyclopropylbutane.

Isomerism

Compounds with the same molecular formula but different properties.

Structural Isomerism

  1. Chain Isomerism: Different carbon skeletons, e.g., n-pentane, isopentane.
  2. Position Isomerism: Different functional group positions, e.g., 1-chloropropane, 2-chloropropane.
  3. Functional Isomerism: Different functional groups, e.g., propanal, propanone.
  4. Metamerism: Different alkyl groups on either side of a functional group, e.g., methoxypropane, diethyl ether.
  5. Tautomerism: Keto-enol forms in carbonyl compounds with α-H, e.g., acetaldehyde.

Stereoisomerism

  1. Optical Isomerism: Enantiomers differ in plane-polarized light rotation (dextrorotatory, laevorotatory). Chiral compounds (with chiral carbon) show this. Number of optical isomers: 2n (n = chiral carbons).
  2. Geometrical Isomerism: Different spatial arrangements around double bonds, e.g., cis/trans-2-butene. Requires a double bond and different groups on each carbon.

Terms Related to Optical Isomerism

  1. Enantiomers: Non-superimposable mirror images.
  2. Diastereomers: Non-mirror image stereoisomers with different properties.
  3. Meso Form: Molecule with a plane of symmetry, optically inactive.
  4. Racemic Mixture: 1:1 enantiomer mixture, optically inactive.
  5. Atropisomers: Isomers separable due to restricted rotation.
  6. Specific Rotation: [α] = observed rotation / (length × density).

Nomenclature of Enantiomers

  1. D-L Configuration: Based on H position relative to other groups.
  2. Threo-Erythro System: Same groups on same (erythro) or opposite (threo) sides.
  3. R-S System: Clockwise (R) or anticlockwise (S) group sequence based on atomic number priority.

Fission of a Covalent Bond

  1. Homolytic Fission: Produces free radicals, e.g., Cl2 → 2Cl·.
  2. Heterolytic Fission: Produces carbocation and carbanion, e.g., A:B → A+ + B−.

Attacking Reagents

  1. Electrophiles: Electron-deficient, e.g., Cl+, BF3.
  2. Nucleophiles: Electron-rich, e.g., Cl−, NH3.
  3. Ambiphiles: Act as both, e.g., C=O, C≡N.

Reaction Intermediates

  1. Free Radicals: sp2-hybridized, planar, e.g., (C6H5)3C·.
  2. Carbocations: sp2-hybridized, planar, six electrons, e.g., (C6H5)3C+.
  3. Carbanions: sp3-hybridized, pyramidal, eight electrons, e.g., (C6H5)3C−.
  4. Carbenes: Divalent carbon, singlet (sp2, bent) or triplet (sp, linear).
  5. Nitrenes: Monovalent nitrogen, singlet or triplet.
  6. Arynes: Contain a formal C≡C in aromatic rings.

Inductive Effect

Permanent electron shift in polar covalent bonds, e.g., CH3Cl → CH3δ+Clδ−. -I (electron-attracting) and +I (electron-releasing) effects.

Electromeric Effect

Temporary polarity in multiple bonds due to reagent approach, e.g., +E or -E types.

Hyperconjugation

Delocalization of C—H σ electrons, stabilizing alkenes and carbocations.

Resonance Effect

Multiple structures describe a molecule (resonance hybrid). +R (electron-donating) and -R (electron-withdrawing) effects.

Types of Organic Reactions

  1. Addition Reactions: Electrophilic (e.g., alkene + H+) or nucleophilic (e.g., carbonyl + CN−).
  2. Substitution Reactions: Electrophilic or nucleophilic (SN1, SN2).
  3. Elimination Reactions: α- or β-elimination forming unsaturated compounds.
  4. Rearrangement Reactions: Atom/group migration, e.g., Hofmann bromamide reaction.

Chapter 25: Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons

Compounds of carbon and hydrogen, e.g., alkanes, alkenes, alkynes.

Alkanes

Saturated hydrocarbons (CnH2n+2), e.g., CH4, C2H6. Inert, called paraffins. Show chain and position isomerism.

Structure

Only σ bonds, tetrahedral (109.5° H—C—H angles).

Methods of Preparation

  1. Hydrogenation: Alkene/alkyne + H2 → alkane.
  2. Sodalime Decarboxylation: RCOONa + NaOH/CaO → R—H + Na2CO3.
  3. Wurtz Reaction: 2RX + 2Na → R—R + 2NaX.
  4. Reduction of Alkyl Halides: RX + 2[H] → R—H + HX.
  5. Kolbe’s Electrolysis: 2CH3COONa + 2H2O → C2H6 + 2CO2 + H2.
  6. Clemmensen’s Reduction: Carbonyl → alkane.
  7. Wolff-Kishner Reduction: Carbonyl → alkane.
  8. From Carbides: Al4C3 + 12H2O → 3CH4.
  9. Corey-House Synthesis: RX + R’CuLi → R—R’ + CuX + LiX.

Physical Properties

First four alkanes are gases, next thirteen liquids, higher solids. Boiling point decreases with branching. Non-polar, soluble in non-polar solvents.

Chemical Properties

  1. Halogenation: CH4 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + HCl, etc.
  2. Combustion: CnH2n+2 + (3n+1)/2 O2 → nCO2 + (n+1)H2O.
  3. Controlled Oxidation: Produces methanol, formaldehyde, or carbon black.
  4. Isomerisation: Rearranges alkane structure.
  5. Aromatisation: Converts to aromatic compounds.
  6. Reaction with Steam: CH4 + H2O → CO + 3H2.
  7. Pyrolysis: Breaks into smaller hydrocarbons.
  8. Nitration: C6H13H + HNO3 → C6H13NO2 + H2O.
  9. Sulphonation: C6H13H + H2SO4 → C6H13SO3H + H2O.

Conformations

Rotation about C—C bonds yields eclipsed (least stable) and staggered (most stable) forms.

Alkenes

Unsaturated hydrocarbons (CnH2n), sp2-hybridized, 120° bond angles, e.g., C2H4.

Structure

Contain π bond, triangular planar shape.

Isomerism

Chain, position, and geometrical (cis-trans) isomerism.

Methods of Preparation

  1. From Alkynes: R—C≡C—R’ + H2 → R—CH=CH—R’.
  2. Dehydrohalogenation: RX → alkene + HX.
  3. From Vicinal Dihalides: RCHXCHXR → RCH=CHR + ZnX2.
  4. Acidic Dehydrogenation: Alcohol → alkene + H2O.

Physical Properties

Similar to alkanes, C1-C3 gases, next fourteen liquids, higher solids.

Chemical Properties

  1. Addition of Halogens: CH2=CH2 + Br2 → CH2BrCH2Br.
  2. Addition of Hydrogen Halides: Follows Markownikoff’s rule, except HBr with peroxide (anti-Markownikoff).
  3. Addition of Sulphuric Acid: CH3CH=CH2 + H2SO4 → CH3CH(OSO3H)CH3.
  4. Addition of Water: CH3C(CH3)=CH2 + H2O → (CH3)3COH.
  5. Oxymercuration-Demercuration: Hydration following Markownikoff’s rule.
  6. Hydroboration-Oxidation: Produces primary alcohols via syn-addition.
  7. Oxidation: Decolourises Baeyer’s reagent, forms diols, ketones, or acids.
  8. Ozonolysis: Cleaves double bonds, forms carbonyls.
  9. Polymerisation: Forms polymers like polythene.
  10. Reaction with Sulphur Monochloride: Forms mustard gas.
  11. Diels-Alder Reaction: Conjugated dienes form cyclohexene derivatives.
  12. Substitution Reactions: Occur at allylic position at high temperatures.

Alkynes

Unsaturated hydrocarbons (CnH2n-2), e.g., C2H2.

Structure

Contain 3σ and 2π bonds, 180° bond angle.

Methods of Preparation

  1. From Calcium Carbide: CaC2 + H2O → C2H2.
  2. From Vicinal Dihalides: RCHXCHXR → R—C≡C—R.
  3. From Tetrahalides: RCHX2CHX2R → R—C≡C—R.

Physical Properties

First two gases, next eight liquids, higher solids. Insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents.

Chemical Properties

  1. Acidic Character: Terminal alkynes form sodium salts, e.g., HC≡CH + Na → HC≡CNa.
  2. Electrophilic Addition: Forms vinyl cations, follows Markownikoff’s rule.
  3. Cyclic Polymerisation: Forms benzene or divinyl acetylene.
  4. Reaction with AsCl3: Forms lewisite (poisonous gas).
  5. Oxidation: Forms acids or diketones.
  6. Ozonolysis: Cleaves triple bonds, forms carbonyls or acids.
  7. Linear Polymerisation: Forms divinyl acetylene.

Benzene

Aromatic hydrocarbon (C6H6), resonance-stabilized hexagonal ring.

Structure

Alternate single and double bonds, per Kekule.

Aromaticity

Planar, delocalized π electrons, follows Huckel’s rule (4n + 2 π electrons).

Methods of Preparation

  1. Cyclic Polymerisation of Ethyne: 3C2H2 → C6H6.
  2. Decarboxylation: C6H5COONa + NaOH/CaO → C6H6 + Na2CO3.

Physical Properties

Colourless liquid/solid, immiscible with water, burns with sooty flame.

Chemical Reactions

Electrophilic substitution via electrophile generation, carbocation formation, and proton removal. Examples: nitration, halogenation, sulphonation, Friedel-Crafts alkylation/acylation.

Directive Influence

Substituents direct incoming groups: o,p-directing (+R, e.g., NH2), m-directing (-R, e.g., NO2).

Carcinogenicity and Toxicity

Benzene and polynuclear hydrocarbons (e.g., 1,2-benzanthracene) are toxic and carcinogenic.

Petroleum

Dark oily liquid containing alkanes, cycloalkanes, and aromatics. Fractionated into gases, naphtha, kerosene, heavy oil, residual oil, and petroleum coke.

LPG and CNG

LPG: Butane and isobutane mixture with ethyl mercaptan. CNG: 95% methane, nearly complete combustion.

Artificial Methods for Petrol

  1. Bergius Process: Coal + H2 → hydrocarbons (60% yield).
  2. Fischer-Tropsch Process: CO + H2 → hydrocarbons.

Octane and Cetane Number

Octane number: % iso-octane in a mixture matching fuel’s antiknock properties. Cetane number: % cetane in a mixture matching diesel’s ignition properties.

Introduction

Haloalkanes and haloarenes are organic compounds containing halogen atoms bonded to carbon. Haloalkanes have halogens attached to an aliphatic carbon chain, while haloarenes feature halogens bonded to an aromatic ring. These compounds are vital in organic synthesis, pharmaceuticals, and industrial applications due to their unique reactivity. This chapter explores their classification, preparation, properties, and reactions, emphasizing the role of the carbon-halogen (C-X) bond.

26.1 Introduction to Haloalkanes and Haloarenes

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define haloalkanes and haloarenes
  • Understand their importance in organic chemistry
  • Recognize their general structure

Haloalkanes (alkyl halides) are alkanes with one or more halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I) replacing hydrogen, e.g., \(\ce{CH3Cl}\) (chloromethane). Haloarenes (aryl halides) have halogens attached to an aromatic ring, e.g., \(\ce{C6H5Cl}\) (chlorobenzene). Their reactivity stems from the polar C-X bond, making them versatile in nucleophilic substitution and elimination reactions.

Key Terms

haloalkane
Organic compound with halogen attached to an aliphatic carbon.
haloarene
Organic compound with halogen attached to an aromatic ring.
C-X bond
Carbon-halogen bond, typically polar due to electronegativity differences.

26.2 Classification of Halogen Derivatives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Differentiate between haloalkanes and haloarenes
  • Identify vinylic and allylic halides

Haloalkanes are classified based on the number of alkyl groups attached to the carbon bearing the halogen:

  • Primary (1°): One alkyl group, e.g., \(\ce{CH3CH2Cl}\) (1-chloroethane).
  • Secondary (2°): Two alkyl groups, e.g., \(\ce{(CH3)2CHCl}\) (2-chloropropane).
  • Tertiary (3°): Three alkyl groups, e.g., \(\ce{(CH3)3CCl}\) (2-chloro-2-methylpropane).

Haloarenes have halogens on aromatic rings. Vinylic halides have halogens on \(\ce{sp^2}\) carbons (e.g., \(\ce{CH2=CHCl}\)), and allylic halides have halogens on carbons adjacent to a double bond (e.g., \(\ce{CH2=CHCH2Cl}\)).

primary haloalkane

vinylic halide

allylic halide
Halide with halogen on a carbon adjacent to a double bond.

26.3 Nature of C-X Bond

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain the polarity of the C-X bond
  • Describe bond strength and length
  • Understand the effect of halogen size

The C-X bond is polar due to the electronegativity difference between carbon and halogens (F: 4.0, Cl: 3.0, Br: 2.8, I: 2.5). The bond dipole moment (\(\mu\)) is:

\[ \mu = q \cdot d \]

Bond length increases (C-F < C-Cl < C-Br < C-I) and bond strength decreases (C-F > C-Cl > C-Br > C-I) as halogen size increases, affecting reactivity.

Key Terms

C-X bond
Polar covalent bond between carbon and a halogen.
bond length
Distance between carbon and halogen nuclei.
bond strength
Energy required to break the C-X bond.

Chapter 1: Quadratic Equations & Miscellaneous Equations

1.1 Roots of Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation has the form \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \). The roots are found using the quadratic formula: \( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \). The discriminant \( b^2 - 4ac \) determines the nature of roots: real and distinct if positive, equal if zero, or imaginary if negative.

Example 1

Solve: (a) \( 2x^2 + 5x - 12 = 0 \), (b) \( x^2 + 11 = 7x \).

(a) Factors: \( (2x - 3)(x + 4) = 0 \), so \( x = \frac{3}{2}, -4 \).

(b) Rewrite: \( x^2 - 7x + 11 = 0 \). Using the formula: \( x = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 44}}{2} \approx 4.618, 2.382 \).

1.2 Sum and Product of Roots

For a quadratic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \), if the roots are \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \), then sum of roots: \( \alpha + \beta = -\frac{b}{a} \), product of roots: \( \alpha \beta = \frac{c}{a} \).

Example 5

If one root is three times the other, find the relation. Let roots be \( \alpha, 3\alpha \). Then: \( 4\alpha = -\frac{b}{a} \), \( 3\alpha^2 = \frac{c}{a} \). Solving gives: \( 3b^2 = 16ac \).

1.3 Miscellaneous Equations Involving One Unknown

Equations reducible to quadratics through substitution or squaring are covered.

Example 7

Solve: \( \sqrt{3 - x} - \sqrt{7 + x} = \sqrt{16 + 2x} \). Squaring both sides and simplifying yields \( 2x^2 + 10x - 12 = 0 \), so \( x = -6 \) (verified).

1.4 Simultaneous Equations

Solve pairs of equations where at least one is quadratic using substitution or elimination.

Example 9

Solve: \( xy = 10 \), \( 3x + 2y = 16 \). Substitute \( y = \frac{10}{x} \) into the second equation: \( 3x + \frac{20}{x} = 16 \). Multiply by \( x \): \( 3x^2 - 16x + 20 = 0 \). Solutions: \( x = \frac{10}{3}, y = 3 \) or \( x = 2, y = 5 \).

1.5 Square Root of (a + √b)

Compute expressions like \( \sqrt{a + \sqrt{b}} \) by assuming \( \sqrt{a + \sqrt{b}} = \pm(\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{y}) \) and solving resulting equations.

Example 11

Find \( \sqrt{14 + 6\sqrt{5}} \). Assume \( \sqrt{14 + 6\sqrt{5}} = \sqrt{x} + \sqrt{y} \). Then: \( x + y = 14 \), \( xy = 45 \). Solutions: \( x = 9, y = 5 \) or \( x = 5, y = 9 \), so \( \pm(3 + \sqrt{5}) \).

Key Terms

Quadratic Equation, Discriminant, Roots, Simultaneous Equations, Irrational Expressions

Review Questions

  1. Solve the quadratic equation \( 8x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0 \).
  2. Find the condition for \( kx(1 - x) = 1 \) to have no real roots.
  3. Solve the simultaneous equations \( x^2 + y = 7 \), \( x + y^2 = 11 \).

Chapter 2: Indices, Logarithms, Remainder Theorem, Partial Fractions

2.1 Fundamental Laws for Positive Integral Indices

Index laws: \( a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n} \), \( a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n} \), \( (a^m)^n = a^{mn} \).

Example 1

Express \( \frac{2b^{-3} c^2}{7c^{-1/2} b^{2/3}} \) with positive indices: \( \frac{2c^{5/2}}{7b^{7/3}} \).

2.2 Fractional, Zero, and Negative Indices

Definitions: \( a^{p/q} = \sqrt[q]{a^p} \), \( a^0 = 1 \), \( a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \).

Example 2

Simplify \( 8^{2/3} \): \( 8^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{8})^2 = 2^2 = 4 \).

2.3 Theory of Logarithms

If \( a^x = N \), then \( x = \log_a N \). Properties: \( \log_a (MN) = \log_a M + \log_a N \), \( \log_a (M/N) = \log_a M - \log_a N \), \( \log_a (M^p) = p \log_a M \).

Example 3

Prove: \( \log_b N = \frac{\log_a N}{\log_a b} \). Let \( \log_b N = x \), so \( b^x = N \). Take \( \log_a \): \( x \log_a b = \log_a N \), thus \( x = \frac{\log_a N}{\log_a b} \).

2.4 Common Logarithms

Base-10 logarithms are used for calculations. \( \log 10 = 1 \), \( \log 1 = 0 \).

Example 5

Given \( \log 2 = 0.3010 \), find \( \log 5 \). Since \( 5 = \frac{10}{2} \), \( \log 5 = \log 10 - \log 2 = 1 - 0.3010 = 0.6990 \).

2.5 Equations with Unknown as Index

Solve using logarithms or index laws.

Example 7

Solve: \( 2^{x^2} = 16^{x-1} \). Since \( 16 = 2^4 \), rewrite: \( 2^{x^2} = 2^{4(x-1)} \). Equate exponents: \( x^2 = 4x - 4 \), so \( x^2 - 4x + 4 = 0 \), \( x = 2 \).

2.6 Remainder Theorem

For a polynomial \( P(x) \) divided by \( x - a \), the remainder is \( P(a) \).

Example 10

Find \( k \): \( 2x^4 + kx^3 - 11x^2 + 4x + 12 \) divided by \( x - 3 \) gives remainder 60. Compute \( P(3) = 60 \): \( 2(3^4) + k(3^3) - 11(3^2) + 4(3) + 12 = 60 \), so \( k = -1 \).

2.7 Undetermined Coefficients

Equate coefficients of equal polynomials to find unknowns.

Example 12

Find \( a, b, c \): \( 2x^2 - 9x + 14 = a(x-1)(x-2) + b(x-1) + c \). Expand and equate: \( a = 2, b = -3, c = 7 \).

2.8 Partial Fractions

Decompose fractions into sums of simpler fractions.

Example 14

Resolve: \( \frac{5}{x^2 + x - 6} \). Factor denominator: \( (x - 2)(x + 3) \). Assume \( \frac{5}{(x-2)(x+3)} = \frac{A}{x-2} + \frac{B}{x+3} \). Solve: \( A = 1, B = -1 \), so \( \frac{1}{x-2} - \frac{1}{x+3} \).

Key Terms

Indices, Logarithms, Remainder Theorem, Partial Fractions, Polynomial Division

Review Questions

  1. Simplify \( \frac{6^{-3} x^{-2}}{4x} \) with positive indices.
  2. Solve \( 3^x - 3^{-x} = 6.832 \) using logarithms.
  3. Resolve \( \frac{5(x+1)}{25 - x^2} \) into partial fractions.

Chapter 3: Progressions, Permutations, Combinations, Binomial Theorem

3.1 Series

A series is a sequence of terms following a specific law, such as arithmetic, geometric, or others like squares of integers.

3.2 Arithmetical Progression

An arithmetic progression (A.P.) has a constant difference \( d \). nth term: \( a + (n-1)d \). Sum: \( s_n = \frac{n}{2} [2a + (n-1)d] \).

Example 1

Insert seven arithmetic means between 2 and 26. Difference: \( d = \frac{26-2}{8} = 3 \). Terms: 5, 8, 11, 14, 17, 20, 23.

3.3 Geometrical Progression

A geometric progression (G.P.) has a constant ratio \( r \). nth term: \( ar^{n-1} \). Sum: \( s_n = \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r} \) (for \( r \neq 1 \)).

Example 4

Insert three geometric means between 162 and 1250. Ratio: \( r = \pm \sqrt[4]{\frac{1250}{162}} = \pm \frac{5}{3} \). Terms: \( \pm 270, 450, \pm 750 \).

3.4 Simple and Compound Interest

Simple interest: \( A = P(1 + \frac{nr}{100}) \). Compound interest: \( A = P(1 + \frac{r}{100})^n \).

Example 7

Calculate £400 at 4% compound interest for 10 years: (i) annual: \( 400 \times (1.04)^{10} \approx 591.7 \), (ii) semi-annual: \( 400 \times (1.02)^{20} \approx 594.4 \).

3.5 Convergence of Geometric Series

A geometric series converges if \( -1 < r < 1 \), sum: \( \frac{a}{1 - r} \).

Example 9

Find \( a, r \) if sum is \( k \), sum of squares is \( l \): \( r = \frac{k^2 - l}{k^2 + l} \), \( a = k(1 - r) \).

3.6 Series of Natural Numbers

Sum of first \( n \): \( \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \). Sum of squares: \( \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \).

Example 10

Evaluate 0.6 as a fraction: \( 0.6 = \frac{6}{10} + \frac{6}{100} + \cdots = \frac{\frac{6}{10}}{1 - \frac{1}{10}} = \frac{2}{3} \).

3.7 Permutations and Combinations

Permutations: \( nPr = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} \). Combinations: \( nCr = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} \).

Example 12

Number of 4-digit numbers from 9 digits: \( 9P4 = \frac{9!}{5!} = 3024 \).

3.8 Probability

Probability: \( \frac{m}{m+n} \), where \( m \) is favorable outcomes, \( n \) is unfavorable.

Example 16

Chance of 13 red cards from 52: \( \frac{^{26}C_{13}}{^{52}C_{13}} \approx \frac{1}{61055} \).

3.9 Binomial Theorem (Positive Integral Index)

\( (1 + x)^n = \sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r} x^r \).

Example 17

Expand \( (x + 3y)^6 \). Term: \( \binom{6}{r} x^{6-r} (3y)^r \). Evaluate \( (103)^6 = (100 + 3)^6 \approx 1.19405 \times 10^{12} \) (5 decimals).

3.10 Binomial Theorem (Non-Positive Index)

For \( -1 < x < 1 \): \( (1 + x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \cdots \).

Example 21

Expand \( \frac{1 + x}{(1 + x)(1 + x^2)} \) to \( x^4 \): Simplifies to \( 7 - 6x - x^2 + 7x^4 + \cdots \).

Key Terms

Arithmetic Progression, Geometric Progression, Permutations, Combinations, Binomial Theorem

Review Questions

  1. Find the 8th term of a series with nth term \( 4n - 5 \).
  2. Sum 10 terms of an A.P. with first term 60, last term -104.
  3. Calculate the probability of three heads or tails alternately in three coin tosses.

Chapter 4: Trigonometrical Ratios for Any Angle, Graphs, Equations

4.1 Introduction

Students are assumed familiar with circular measure and trigonometrical ratios for acute angles. Thus, \( x \text{ degrees} = \frac{\pi x}{180} \text{ radians} \). For a right-angled triangle \( \triangle ABC \) with \( \angle BCA = 90^\circ \), \( \angle ABC = \theta \), base \( x \), height \( y \), and hypotenuse \( r \):

\[ \sin \theta = \frac{y}{r}, \cos \theta = \frac{x}{r}, \tan \theta = \frac{y}{x}, \]

\[ \csc \theta = \frac{r}{y}, \sec \theta = \frac{r}{x}, \cot \theta = \frac{x}{y}. \]

Key relations include:

\[ \tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}, \quad \sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1, \]

\[ 1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta, \quad 1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta. \]

Example 1

If \( \sin \theta = \frac{1}{3} \), find other ratios. Using \( \cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta = \frac{8}{9} \), so \( \cos \theta = \pm \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3} \). Then, \( \tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}} \), \( \csc \theta = 3 \), \( \sec \theta = \frac{3}{2\sqrt{2}} \), \( \cot \theta = 2\sqrt{2} \).

Example 2

Show: \( \sin^3 \theta - \cos^3 \theta = (\sin \theta - \cos \theta)(1 + \sin \theta \cos \theta) \).

\[ \sin^3 \theta - \cos^3 \theta = (\sin \theta - \cos \theta)(\sin^2 \theta + \sin \theta \cos \theta + \cos^2 \theta) = (\sin \theta - \cos \theta)(1 + \sin \theta \cos \theta). \]

4.2 Trigonometrical Ratios for the General Angle

A radius \( OP \) rotates from initial position \( OX \), sweeping positive angles (anti-clockwise) or negative angles (clockwise). For angle \( \theta \), with \( P(x, y) \), \( r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \):

\[ \sin \theta = \frac{y}{r}, \cos \theta = \frac{x}{r}, \tan \theta = \frac{y}{x}, \]

\[ \csc \theta = \frac{r}{y}, \sec \theta = \frac{r}{x}, \cot \theta = \frac{x}{y}. \]

Signs depend on quadrant: 1st (all positive), 2nd (\( \sin, \csc \) positive), 3rd (\( \tan, \cot \) positive), 4th (\( \cos, \sec \) positive).

4.3 Trigonometrical Ratios of Some Related Angles

Relations for related angles:

\[ \sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta, \cos(-\theta) = \cos \theta, \tan(-\theta) = -\tan \theta, \]

\[ \sin(180^\circ \pm \theta) = \mp \sin \theta, \cos(180^\circ \pm \theta) = -\cos \theta, \tan(180^\circ \pm \theta) = \pm \tan \theta, \]

\[ \sin(90^\circ \pm \theta) = \cos \theta, \cos(90^\circ \pm \theta) = \mp \sin \theta, \tan(90^\circ \pm \theta) = \mp \cot \theta, \]

\[ \sin(360^\circ \pm \theta) = \pm \sin \theta, \cos(360^\circ \pm \theta) = \cos \theta. \]

Example 3

Express: \( \sin 135^\circ = \sin(180^\circ - 45^\circ) = \sin 45^\circ \), \( \tan 140^\circ = \tan(180^\circ - 40^\circ) = -\tan 40^\circ \),

\[ \sin 1220^\circ = \sin(3 \times 360^\circ + 140^\circ) = \sin 140^\circ = \sin 40^\circ, \]

\[ \cos(-840^\circ) = \cos 840^\circ = \cos(2 \times 360^\circ + 120^\circ) = -\cos 60^\circ. \]

4.4 Graphs of Trigonometrical Ratios for Acute Angles

The graph of \( \sin \theta \) rises from 0 to 1 as \( \theta \) goes from 0° to 90°. \( \cos \theta \) mirrors \( \sin(90^\circ - \theta) \). \( \tan \theta \) starts at 0 and increases rapidly toward 90°. Specific values:

\[ \sin 45^\circ = \cos 45^\circ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \tan 45^\circ = 1, \]

\[ \sin 60^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \cos 60^\circ = \frac{1}{2}, \tan 60^\circ = \sqrt{3}, \]

\[ \sin 30^\circ = \frac{1}{2}, \cos 30^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \tan 30^\circ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}. \]

4.5 Graphs of Trigonometrical Ratios for the General Angle

Using relations from 4.3, graphs extend to all angles. \( \sin \theta \) and \( \cos \theta \) have period 360°, amplitude 1. \( \tan \theta \) has period 180°. Relations like \( \sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1 \) hold for all \( \theta \).

Example 4

Graph \( y = \sin(2x + \frac{2\pi}{3}) \) from \( x = 0 \) to \( x = \pi \). Solve \( x = 5 \sin(2x + \frac{2\pi}{3}) \). Intersections at \( x \approx 0.48, 2.34, 3.30 \).

4.6 Solution of Trigonometrical Equations

Equations often reduce to \( \sin \theta = c \), \( \cos \theta = c \), or \( \tan \theta = c \). General solutions:

\[ \sin \theta = c: \theta = n \pi + (-1)^n \sin^{-1} c, \]

\[ \cos \theta = c: \theta = 2n \pi \pm \cos^{-1} c, \]

\[ \tan \theta = c: \theta = n \pi + \tan^{-1} c. \]

Example 5

Solve: \( 2 \cos^2 \theta = 1 + \sin \theta \). Rewrite: \( 2 \sin^2 \theta + \sin \theta - 1 = 0 \). Solutions: \( \sin \theta = \frac{1}{2} \) (\( \theta = 30^\circ, 150^\circ \)) or \( \sin \theta = -1 \) (\( \theta = 270^\circ \)).

Example 6

Solve: \( 4 \sec^2 \theta = 3 \tan \theta + 5 \). Rewrite: \( 4 \tan^2 \theta - 3 \tan \theta - 1 = 0 \). Solutions: \( \tan \theta = 1 \) (\( \theta = n \pi + \frac{\pi}{4} \)) or \( \tan \theta = -\frac{1}{4} \) (\( \theta = n \pi - 14^\circ 2' \)).

Example 7

Solve: \( \sin 2\theta = \cos 3\theta \). Rewrite: \( \sin 2\theta = \sin(90^\circ - 3\theta) \). Solutions: \( \theta = 18^\circ, 90^\circ, 162^\circ, 234^\circ, 270^\circ, 306^\circ \).

Example 8

Solve: \( \tan \theta = \sqrt{3}, \sec \theta = -2 \). Solutions: \( \theta = n \cdot 360^\circ + 240^\circ \).

Key Terms

Trigonometrical Ratios, Quadrants, Period, Amplitude, Inverse Functions

Review Questions

  1. If \( \sin \theta = \frac{1}{4} \), find other ratios.
  2. Solve \( 2 \tan \theta + 3 \sec \theta = 4 \cos \theta \).
  3. Graph \( \sin 2x \) and \( 1 - \cos x \) for \( 0^\circ \leq x \leq 180^\circ \). Solve \( \sin 2x = 1 - \cos x \).

Chapter 5: Addition Theorems, Multiple Angles, Inverse Notation, Small Angles

5.1 Addition Theorems for Sine and Cosine

For angles \( A, B \):

\[ \sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B, \]

\[ \cos(A + B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B, \]

\[ \sin(A - B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B, \]

\[ \cos(A - B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B. \]

Example 1

Show: \( \cos 15^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{2}(\sqrt{3} + 1)}{4} \).

\[ \cos 15^\circ = \cos(45^\circ - 30^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{2}(\sqrt{3} + 1)}{4}. \]

Example 2

Show: \( \cos(90^\circ + A) = -\sin A \).

\[ \cos(90^\circ + A) = \cos 90^\circ \cos A - \sin 90^\circ \sin A = 0 \cdot \cos A - 1 \cdot \sin A = -\sin A. \]

5.2 Addition Theorem for the Tangent

\[ \tan(A + B) = \frac{\tan A + \tan B}{1 - \tan A \tan B}, \]

\[ \tan(A - B) = \frac{\tan A - \tan B}{1 + \tan A \tan B}. \]

Example 3

Show: \( \tan(45^\circ + A) = \frac{1 + \tan A}{1 - \tan A} \).

\[ \tan(45^\circ + A) = \frac{\tan 45^\circ + \tan A}{1 - \tan 45^\circ \tan A} = \frac{1 + \tan A}{1 - \tan A}. \]

5.3 Multiple Angles

\[ \sin 2A = 2 \sin A \cos A, \]

\[ \cos 2A = \cos^2 A - \sin^2 A = 2 \cos^2 A - 1 = 1 - 2 \sin^2 A, \]

\[ \tan 2A = \frac{2 \tan A}{1 - \tan^2 A}, \]

\[ \sin 3A = 3 \sin A - 4 \sin^3 A, \cos 3A = 4 \cos^3 A - 3 \cos A, \]

\[ \tan 3A = \frac{3 \tan A - \tan^3 A}{1 - 3 \tan^2 A}. \]

5.4 Submultiple Angles

Let \( t = \tan \frac{x}{2} \):

\[ \sin x = \frac{2t}{1 + t^2}, \cos x = \frac{1 - t^2}{1 + t^2}, \tan x = \frac{2t}{1 - t^2}. \]

Example 4

Prove: \( \frac{\cos \theta}{\cos 2\theta} - \cos 3\theta = 2 (\cos 2\theta - \cos 4\theta) \).

Using multiple angle formulas, simplify to \( 2(\cos 2\theta - \cos 4\theta) \).

Example 5

Chapter 10: Introduction to the Ideas of the Integral Calculus

10.1 The Nature of the Fundamental Problem of the Integral Calculus

The differential calculus focuses on the rate of variation of known functions, whereas the integral calculus addresses the inverse problem: finding a function \( y \) given its derivative \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \phi(x) \), where \( \phi(x) \) is a known function. For example, if the velocity of a particle is \( u + at \), where \( u \) and \( a \) are constants, we need to find the distance \( s \) from \( \frac{ds}{dt} = u + at \). The solution is \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 + C \), where \( C \) is an arbitrary constant. This is written as the indefinite integral:

\[ s = \int (u + at) \, dt = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 + C. \]

Integration is an inverse operation, and the presence of the arbitrary constant \( C \) indicates multiple possible solutions. The indefinite integral \( y = \int \phi(x) \, dx \) represents all functions whose derivative is \( \phi(x) \).

10.2 Standard Forms

Integration, being an inverse operation, relies on known derivatives. A list of standard integrals is derived from standard derivatives (see page 147):

\[ \int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C, \quad (n \neq -1), \]

\[ \int \cos x \, dx = \sin x + C, \quad \int \sin x \, dx = -\cos x + C, \]

\[ \int \sec^2 x \, dx = \tan x + C, \quad \int \csc^2 x \, dx = -\cot x + C, \]

\[ \int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}} = \sin^{-1} \frac{x}{a} + C, \quad \int \frac{dx}{a^2 + x^2} = \frac{1}{a} \tan^{-1} \frac{x}{a} + C. \]

The integral of a sum or difference is the sum or difference of the integrals:

\[ \int [\phi(x) \pm \psi(x)] \, dx = \int \phi(x) \, dx \pm \int \psi(x) \, dx, \]

and for a constant \( a \):

\[ \int a \phi(x) \, dx = a \int \phi(x) \, dx. \]

Example 1

Evaluate \( \int \left( x^2 + \frac{2}{x} + \frac{1}{x^2} \right) dx \):

\[ \int \left( x^2 + \frac{2}{x} + \frac{1}{x^2} \right) dx = \frac{x^3}{3} + 2 \ln |x| - \frac{1}{x} + C. \]

Example 2

\[ (2x - 1)^3 = 8x^3 - 12x^2 + 6x - 1, \quad \int (2x - 1)^3 \, dx = 2x^4 - 4x^3 + 3x^2 - x + C. \]

10.4 Calculation of an Area as a Limit of a Sum

Consider the area under \( y = 1 + x \) from \( x = 0 \) to \( x = 10 \). Divide into \( n \) strips of width \( \frac{10}{n} \). For the \( r \)-th strip, the left edge is at \( x = (r-1)\frac{10}{n} \), height \( y = 1 + (r-1)\frac{10}{n} \), and right edge height \( 1 + \frac{10r}{n} \). The area is between:

\[ \sum_{r=1}^n \frac{10}{n} \left( 1 + (r-1)\frac{10}{n} \right) = 60 - \frac{50}{n}, \quad \sum_{r=1}^n \frac{10}{n} \left( 1 + \frac{10r}{n} \right) = 60 + \frac{50}{n}. \]

As \( n \to \infty \), the area approaches 60 units.

10.5 The Integral as a Sum

For a general curve \( y = \phi(x) \) from \( x = a \) to \( x = b \), divide into \( n \) strips of width \( \delta x = \frac{b-a}{n} \). The area of a strip is approximately \( y \delta x \). The total area is the limit:

\[ \int_a^b y \, dx = \lim_{\delta x \to 0} \sum y \delta x = A(b) - A(a), \]

where \( A(x) = \int \phi(x) \, dx \).

10.6 The Example of § 10.4 Solved by Integration

For \( y = 1 + x \), \( x = 0 \) to \( x = 10 \):

\[ \int_0^{10} (1 + x) \, dx = \left[ x + \frac{x^2}{2} \right]_0^{10} = 10 + \frac{100}{2} = 60. \]

11.1 Introduction

Integration extends beyond standard forms, requiring systematic methods. Some functions cannot be integrated in elementary terms, necessitating experience and judgment.

11.2 Generalisation of the List of Standard Integrals

If \( \int f'(x) \, dx = f(x) + C \), then for constants \( a, b \):

\[ \int f'(ax + b) \, dx = \frac{1}{a} f(ax + b) + C. \]

Generalized standard integrals:

\[ \int (ax + b)^n \, dx = \frac{(ax + b)^{n+1}}{(n+1)a} + C, \quad (n \neq -1), \]

\[ \int \sin ax \, dx = -\frac{1}{a} \cos ax + C, \quad \int \cos ax \, dx = \frac{1}{a} \sin ax + C, \]

\[ \int \sec^2 ax \, dx = \frac{1}{a} \tan ax + C, \quad \int \csc^2 ax \, dx = -\frac{1}{a} \cot ax + C, \]

\[ \int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}} = \sin^{-1} \frac{x}{a} + C, \quad \int \frac{dx}{a^2 + x^2} = \frac{1}{a} \tan^{-1} \frac{x}{a} + C. \]

Example 1

Evaluate \( \int_0^1 \left( \sqrt{x + 3} + \sqrt{x + 2} \right) dx \):

\[ \int \left( (x + 3)^{1/2} + (x + 2)^{1/2} \right) dx = \frac{2}{3} (x + 3)^{3/2} + \frac{2}{3} (x + 2)^{3/2} + C, \]

\[ \left[ \frac{2}{3} (x + 3)^{3/2} + \frac{2}{3} (x + 2)^{3/2} \right]_0^1 = \frac{8}{3} - \frac{4\sqrt{3}}{3}. \]

Example 2

Evaluate \( \int_0^{\pi/2} (\cos x + 2 \cos 2x) \, dx \):

\[ \int (\cos x + 2 \cos 2x) \, dx = \sin x + \sin 2x + C, \quad \left[ \sin x + \sin 2x \right]_0^{\pi/2} = 1. \]

Example 3

\[ \left[ \frac{1}{3} \tan^{-1} \frac{x - 2}{3} \right]_0^2 = \frac{1}{3} \tan^{-1} 0 - \frac{1}{3} \tan^{-1} \left( -\frac{2}{3} \right) = \frac{1}{3} \tan^{-1} \frac{2}{3}. \]

11.4 Integration by Change of Variable

Change variable from \( x \) to \( t \):

\[ \int \phi(x) \, dx = \int \phi(x(t)) \frac{dx}{dt} \, dt. \]

Example 8

Integrate \( x \sqrt{1 + x^2} \):

\[ \int x \sqrt{1 + x^2} \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \int \sqrt{1 + t} \, dt = \frac{1}{3} (1 + t)^{3/2} + C = \frac{1}{3} (1 + x^2)^{3/2} + C. \]

Example 9

Find \( \int \frac{x \, dx}{1 + x^4} \):

Let \( x^2 = t \), \( dx = \frac{dt}{2x} \):

\[ \int \frac{x}{1 + x^4} \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{dt}{1 + t^2} = \frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1} t + C = \frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1} x^2 + C. \]

Example 10

Find \( \int \sin^3 x \cos x \, dx \):

Let \( \sin x = t \), \( \cos x \, dx = dt \):

\[ \int \sin^3 x \cos x \, dx = \int t^3 \, dt = \frac{t^4}{4} + C = \frac{\sin^4 x}{4} + C. \]

Example 11

Integrate \( \cos^5 x \):

Let \( \sin x = t \), \( \cos x \, dx = dt \), \( \cos^4 x = (1 - \sin^2 x)^2 \):

\[ \int \cos^5 x \, dx = \int (1 - t^2)^2 \, dt = \int (1 - 2t^2 + t^4) \, dt = t - \frac{2}{3} t^3 + \frac{1}{5} t^5 + C = \sin x - \frac{2}{3} \sin^3 x + \frac{1}{5} \sin^5 x + C. \]

11.5 Definite Integrals by Change of Variable

For definite integrals, adjust limits or revert to original variable after integration.

Example 13

Evaluate \( \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^3 x \, dx \):

Method 1: \( \cos x = t \), \( -\sin x \, dx = dt \), \( \int \sin^3 x \, dx = -\int (1 - t^2) \, dt = -t + \frac{t^3}{3} + C = -\cos x + \frac{1}{3} \cos^3 x + C, \]

\[ \left[ -\cos x + \frac{1}{3} \cos^3 x \right]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{2}{3}. \]

Method 2: Limits \( x = 0 \implies t = 1 \), \( x = \frac{\pi}{2} \implies t = 0 \), \( \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^3 x \, dx = -\int_1^0 (1 - t^2) \, dt = \int_0^1 (1 - t^2) \, dt = \frac{2}{3}. \]

Example 14

Evaluate \( \int_0^1 \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx \), let \( x = \sin t \):

\[ dx = \cos t \, dt \), limits \( x = 0 \implies t = 0 \), \( x = 1 \implies t = \frac{\pi}{2} \),

\[ \int_0^1 \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{\sin t}{\cos t} \cos t \, dt = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin t \, dt = [-\cos t]_0^{\pi/2} = 1. \]

Evaluate \( \int_0^{\pi/4} \sec^4 x \, dx \):

Let \( t = \tan x \), \( \sec^2 x \, dx = dt \), limits \( x = 0 \implies t = 0 \), \( x = \frac{\pi}{4} \implies t = 1 \):

\[ \int_0^{\pi/4} \sec^4 x \, dx = \int_0^1 (1 + t^2) \, dt = \left[ t + \frac{t^3}{3} \right]_0^1 = \frac{4}{3}. \]

11.6 Integration by Parts

\[ \int u \frac{dv}{dx} \, dx = uv - \int v \frac{du}{dx} \, dx. \]

Example 16

Integrate \( x \tan^{-1} x \):

Let \( u = \tan^{-1} x \), \( \frac{dv}{dx} = x \), \( \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \), \( v = \frac{x^2}{2} \):

\[ \int x \tan^{-1} x \, dx = \frac{x^2}{2} \tan^{-1} x - \int \frac{x^2}{2(1 + x^2)} \, dx = \frac{x^2 + 1}{2} \tan^{-1} x - \frac{x}{2} + C. \]

Find \( \int x \cos x \, dx \):

Let \( u = x \), \( \frac{dv}{dx} = \cos x \), \( \frac{du}{dx} = 1 \), \( v = \sin x \):

\[ \int x \cos x \, dx = x \sin x - \int \sin x \, dx = x \sin x + \cos x + C. \]

11.7 Approximate Methods of Integration

When exact integration is not possible, approximate methods like the trapezoidal rule and Simpson’s rule are used.

Trapezoidal rule: \( \int_a^b y \, dx \approx \frac{h}{2} [y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1 + \cdots + y_{n-1})], \quad h = \frac{b-a}{n}. \]

Simpson’s rule (even \( n \)): \( \int_a^b y \, dx \approx \frac{h}{3} [y_0 + y_{2n} + 4(y_1 + y_3 + \cdots + y_{2n-1}) + 2(y_2 + y_4 + \cdots + y_{2n-2})], \quad h = \frac{b-a}{2n}. \]

Simpson’s: \( \frac{0.1}{3} (0.2867 + 4 \cdot 0.4405 + 2 \cdot 0.2955) = 0.08799. \]

14.1 Systems of coordinates

In elementary graphical work, it is customary to specify the position of a point P in a plane by its perpendicular distances OM, MP from two fixed perpendicular lines Oy, Ox (Fig. 91). The lines Ox, Oy are called the axes, O the origin and the distances OM, MP are referred to as the abscissa and ordinate of the point P. A convenient method of referring shortly to the position of a point whose abscissa is x and ordinate y is to say that the point has coordinates x and y and to use the symbol $(x, y)$ to designate the position of such a point.

The student is assumed to be familiar with the usual sign conventions used in graphical work. Briefly these are as follows—for points to the right of the axis Oy the abscissa is positive and for points to the left of Oy the abscissa is negative, while the ordinate is positive or negative according as the point is above or below the axis Ox. Thus, in Fig. 92, the points $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$ would be denoted by $(5,2), (-2,3), (-3,-1)$ and $(3,-2)$ respectively.

The above method of specifying the position of a point in a plane by its distances from two straight lines (the axes) was first introduced by the philosopher Des Cartes. It is not essential for the axes to be mutually perpendicular as in Figs. 91, 92, but it is often convenient to use, and in this book we shall only deal with, axes at right angles. Such a system of coordinates is known as the Cartesian system and, when the axes are at right angles to one another as in Fig. 91, x and y are referred to as the rectangular Cartesian coordinates of the point P. Other coordinate systems, for example that outlined below, exist but the Cartesian system is by far the most important.

Another method of specifying the position of a point in a plane is by its polar coordinates. Suppose that O is a fixed point, called the origin or pole, and Ox is a fixed line, called the initial line. Then in Fig. 93, the position of a point P is known when the angle $xOP (= \theta)$ and the length $OP (=r)$ are given. The two quantities r and $\theta$ are called the polar coordinates of P and the position of P is conveniently denoted in this system of coordinates by the symbol $(r, \theta)$.

14.2 The relation between Cartesian and polar coordinates

Suppose that the rectangular Cartesian coordinates of a point P are $(x, y)$ and that its polar coordinates are $(r, \theta)$. Then, from Fig. 94, $$ x = OM = r \cos\theta, $$ $$ y = MP = r \sin\theta, $$ and x, y can be found when r, $\theta$ are given. Conversely, if x, y are given, we have $$ r^2 = x^2 + y^2, \quad \tan \theta = y/x. \quad (14.2) $$ Equations (14.2) do not determine r, $\theta$ uniquely, for $r = \pm\sqrt{(x^2 + y^2)}$ and $\theta$ can take an indefinite number of different values. To obtain an unique correspondence, we take $r = +\sqrt{(x^2 + y^2)}$ and determine $\theta$ as the angle which lies between $-\pi$ and $+\pi$ satisfying the two equations $\cos \theta = x/r, \sin \theta = y/r$.

Example 1. Find (i) the Cartesian coordinates of the point whose polar coordinates are $(5, \pi/4)$ and (ii) the polar coordinates of the point whose Cartesian coordinates are $(-1,1)$.

(i) $r = 5, \theta = \pi/4$, so that $$ x = 5 \cos (\pi/4) = 5/\sqrt{2} = 3.536, \quad y = 5 \sin (\pi/4) = 5/\sqrt{2} = 3.536. $$ (ii) $x = -1, y = 1$, so that $$ r = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2} = 1.414, \quad \cos \theta = -1/\sqrt{2}, \quad \sin \theta = 1/\sqrt{2} $$ giving $\theta = 3\pi/4$.

14.3 The distance between two points with given rectangular coordinates

Let $P_1, P_2$ be the two given points and let their coordinates be respectively $(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)$. Draw (Fig. 95) $P_1M_1, P_2M_2$ perpendicular to Ox, and draw $P_2R$ parallel to Ox to meet $P_1M_1$ in R. Then $$ P_2R = M_2M_1 = OM_1 - OM_2 = x_1 - x_2, $$ and $$ RP_1 = M_1P_1 - M_1R = M_1P_1 - M_2P_2 = y_1 - y_2. $$ From the right-angled triangle $P_2RP_1$, $$ P_1P_2 = \sqrt{(P_2R^2 + RP_1^2)} = \sqrt{\{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2\}}. \quad (14.3) $$ The proof of formula (14.3) has been given only for the case in which the coordinates of both the points are all positive. When due regard is paid to the usual sign conventions for the coordinates, it will be found to remain true for any positions of the two given points.

Example 2. Find the distance between the points $(4, -7)$ and $(-1, 5)$.

By writing $x_1 = 4, y_1 = -7, x_2 = -1, y_2 = 5$ in formula (14.3), or (preferably) working from first principles from a diagram showing the given points, it will be found that the required distance $$ = \sqrt{\{(4 + 1)^2 + (-7 - 5)^2\}} = \sqrt{(25 + 144)} = \sqrt{(169)} = 13. $$

14.4 A proof of the addition formulae of trigonometry

A compact method of obtaining a formula for $\cos (A - B)$ where A and B are angles of any magnitude can now be given. In Fig. 96, diagrams are given for $90^\circ < A < 180^\circ, 0 < B < 90^\circ$ and $0 < A < 90^\circ, 180^\circ < B < 270^\circ$ and corresponding diagrams can be constructed for angles of any size. If OP and OQ are each of unit length, the Cartesian coordinates of P are $(\cos A, \sin A)$ and of Q $(\cos B, \sin B)$. In both cases $\cos \angle POQ = \cos (A - B)$, and the cosine formula for the triangle POQ gives $$ PQ^2 = OP^2 + OQ^2 - 2(OP)(OQ) \cos \angle POQ = 1 + 1 - 2 \cos (A - B) = 2 - 2 \cos (A - B). $$ But, from formula (14.3) for the distance between P and Q, $$ PQ^2 = (\cos A - \cos B)^2 + (\sin A - \sin B)^2 = 2 - 2 \cos A \cos B - 2 \sin A \sin B, $$ when use is made of the relations $$ \cos^2 A + \sin^2 A = 1, \quad \cos^2 B + \sin^2 B = 1. $$ Equating these values of $PQ^2$ we have $$ \cos (A - B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B. $$ The formulae for $\cos (A + B), \sin (A + B), \sin (A - B)$ can then be deduced by replacing B by $-B, 90^\circ - B$ and $B - 90^\circ$.

14.5 The coordinates of a point which divides the join of two given points in a given ratio

In Fig. 97, let $P_1$ and $P_2$ be the points $(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)$ respectively and suppose we require to find the coordinates of a point P which divides the line $P_1P_2$ in the ratio $m_1 : m_2$. Draw $P_1M_1, P_2M_2, PM$ perpendicular to the axis Ox and draw $P_1S$ parallel to Ox to meet $PM, P_2M_2$ in R and S respectively. Then, if P is the point $(x, y)$, $OM_1 = x_1, OM_2 = x_2, OM = x$. From the construction of Fig. 97 it should be clear that $$ P_1R = M_1M = OM - OM_1 = x - x_1, $$ $$ RS = MM_2 = OM_2 - OM = x_2 - x. $$ Since PR, $P_2S$ are parallel, $$ \frac{P_1R}{RS} = \frac{P_1P}{PP_2} = \frac{m_1}{m_2} $$ so that $$ \frac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x} = \frac{m_1}{m_2} $$ Solving for x we find $$ x = \frac{m_1x_2 + m_2x_1}{m_1 + m_2}. \quad (14.4) $$ By drawing $P_1N_1, PN, P_2N_2$ perpendicular to Oy, we could show in the same way that the ordinate of P is given by $$ y = \frac{m_1y_2 + m_2y_1}{m_1 + m_2}. $$ Hence the coordinates $(x, y)$ of a point P which divides the join of the points $P_1(x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2(x_2, y_2)$ internally in the ratio $m_1 : m_2$ are given by $$ x = \frac{m_1x_2 + m_2x_1}{m_1 + m_2}, \quad y = \frac{m_1y_2 + m_2y_1}{m_1 + m_2}. $$ These are called Joachimsthal’s section-formulae. As a special case, the coordinates of the middle point of the line joining $(x_1, y_1)$ to $(x_2, y_2)$ are $\frac{1}{2}(x_1 + x_2)$ and $\frac{1}{2}(y_1 + y_2)$ for here $m_1 = m_2$.

If the point Q divides the line $P_1P_2$ externally in the ratio $m_1:m_2$, i.e., if $P_1Q:QP_2::m_1:m_2$, its coordinates would be found in a similar way to be $$ x = \frac{m_1x_2 - m_2x_1}{m_1 - m_2}, \quad y = \frac{m_1y_2 - m_2y_1}{m_1 - m_2}. \quad (14.5) $$

Example 3. The vertices of a triangle are $A(2,4), B(-4,-6), C(6,0)$. Write down the coordinates of X, the mid-point of BC. Find also the coordinates of the points which divide AX internally and externally in the ratio 2:1.

The coordinates of the mid-point of BC are given by $$ x = \frac{1}{2}(-4 + 6) = 1, \quad y = \frac{1}{2}(-6 + 0) = -3, $$ so that X is the point $(1, -3)$. The coordinates of the point dividing AX internally in the ratio 2:1 are given by $$ x = \frac{2 \times 1 + 1 \times 2}{2 + 1} = \frac{4}{3}, \quad y = \frac{2 \times (-3) + 1 \times 4}{2 + 1} = -\frac{2}{3}. $$ The coordinates of the point dividing AX externally in the ratio 2:1 are given by $$ x = \frac{2 \times 1 - 1 \times 2}{2 - 1} = 0, \quad y = \frac{2 \times (-3) - 1 \times 4}{2 - 1} = -10. $$

14.6 The area of a triangle whose vertices have given coordinates

In Fig. 98, let the vertices of the triangle ABC be the points $A(x_1, y_1), B(x_2, y_2)$ and $C(x_3, y_3)$. Draw AL, BM, CN perpendicular to the axis Ox. Then area $\triangle ABC$ $$ = \text{area trapezium } ALNC + \text{area trapezium } CNMB - \text{area trapezium } ALMB $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} LN(LA + NC) + \frac{1}{2} NM(NC + MB) - \frac{1}{2} LM(LA + MB) $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}(x_3 - x_1)(y_1 + y_3) + \frac{1}{2}(x_2 - x_3)(y_3 + y_2) - \frac{1}{2}(x_2 - x_1)(y_1 + y_2). $$ After a slight rearrangement of the right-hand side, we have $$ \text{area } \triangle ABC = \frac{1}{2}\{x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)\}. \quad (14.6) $$ By taking the point C to be at the origin O $(x_3 = y_3 = 0)$, the area of the triangle OAB with vertices $O(0,0), A(x_1,y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ is given by $$ \text{area } \triangle OAB = \frac{1}{2}(x_1y_2 - x_2y_1). \quad (14.7) $$ It should be noted that for the formula (14.6) to give a positive value for the area, it is necessary for the points A, B and C to be taken in a special order. This is such that in starting from the point A and proceeding round the perimeter of the triangle in the order A, B and C, the area of the triangle must always be on the left. In a specific example it is probably better to draw a rough diagram and work from first principles (see Example 4).

The above formula for the area of a triangle can be used to obtain the condition that three points shall be collinear (i.e., that they shall lie on the same straight line). If the three points $(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)$ and $(x_3, y_3)$ lie on the same straight line, the area of the triangle formed by them vanishes and, from (14.6) the condition for collinearity is therefore $$ x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2) = 0. \quad (14.8) $$ The area of a quadrilateral (or other plane polygon) with given vertices can be obtained in a similar way. Perpendiculars are dropped from the vertices on to the axis Ox (or on to a line parallel to Ox through the vertex with the smallest ordinate) and the area can then be expressed in terms of the areas of various trapezia (or triangles). The resulting formula is rather complicated and again it is probably preferable to work from first principles (see Example 6).

Example 4. Find the area of the triangle formed by the points $(-2,3), (-7,5), (3, -5)$.

Denoting the points $(-2, 3), (-7,5)$ and $(3, -5)$ by A, B and C, we drop perpendiculars AL, BM on to a line through C parallel to the axis Ox. Then, $$ \text{area } \triangle ABC = \text{area trapezium } BMLA + \text{area } \triangle ALC - \text{area } \triangle BMC $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}(5)(10 + 8) + \frac{1}{2}(5)(8) - \frac{1}{2}(10)(10) = 15 \text{ units}. $$ Alternatively, since in proceeding round the perimeter of the triangle in the order, A, B, C, the area of the triangle is always on the left, we could obtain the same result by writing $x_1 = -2, x_2 = -7, x_3 = 3, y_1 = 3, y_2 = 5, y_3 = -5$ in formula (14.6).

Example 5. Find the relation between x and y if the point $(x, y)$ lies on the line joining the points $(2, 3)$ and $(5,4)$.

The condition (14.8) for collinearity of the three given points gives $$ x(3 - 4) + 2(4 - y) + 5(y - 3) = 0, $$ i.e, $x - 3y + 7 = 0$.

Example 6. Find the area of the quadrilateral whose vertices are the points $(2,1), (3,5), (-3,4)$ and $(-2,-2)$.

In Fig. 100, the four points $(2,1), (3,5), (-3,4), (-2,-2)$ are denoted by A, B, C and D. Perpendiculars AL, BM, CN are dropped from A, B and C on to a line through D (the point with the smallest ordinate) parallel to Ox. Then $$ \text{area } ABCD = \text{area trapezium } CNMB - \text{area } \triangle CND - \text{area } \triangle DLA - \text{area trapezium } ALMB $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}(6)(6 + 7) - \frac{1}{2}(3)(6) - \frac{1}{2}(4)(3) - \frac{1}{2}(1)(3 + 7) = 25 \text{ units}. $$

14.7 The equation to a locus

If a point moves so that it always satisfies a given condition, or conditions, the path traced out by it is called its locus. For example, if a point always lies on the straight line joining the points $(2,3)$ and $(3,4)$ we have seen in Example 5 of this chapter that its coordinates $(x, y)$ always satisfy the equation $$ x - 3y + 7 = 0. \quad (14.9) $$ The locus of the point $(x, y)$ is here the straight line joining the two given points and equation (14.9) is the equation of the locus. (It will be shown in the next chapter that when the locus of the moving point is a straight line, its equation is of the first degree in x and y.)

Again, suppose that a point P moves in a plane so that the lines AP, BP joining it to two fixed points A and B in the same plane are (Fig. 101) always at right angles to each other. Then we know that P lies on the circumference of a circle on AB as diameter (see Fig. 101 in which $P_1, P_2, P_3$ show three possible positions of the point P). The circumference of this circle is the locus of P when its motion is restricted so as to fulfil the above condition.

If a point moves in a plane so as to satisfy some condition such as the above, it will describe in general a definite curve or locus. An equation can in general be found which relates the coordinates $(x, y)$ of the point and this relation will be true only for points lying on the locus. Conversely, to every equation relating x and y there is, in general, a definite geometrical locus. Thus equation (14.9) above represents a straight line. Many other examples will occur to the reader who will already be familiar with the concept of an equation representing a curve from his previous work on graphs and curve sketching.

A few examples on the method of formation of the equation to a locus are appended.

Example 7. Find the equation to the locus of a point which is always equidistant from the two points $(0,0)$ and $(3,4)$.

In Fig. 102, O is the point $(0,0)$ and A the point $(3,4)$. $P_1, P_2, P_3$ are three possible positions of a point P on the required locus. Let $P(x, y)$ be any point on the locus. Then $$ OP^2 = x^2 + y^2, $$ $$ PA^2 = (x - 3)^2 + (y - 4)^2. $$ Since OP is always to be equal to PA, $$ x^2 + y^2 = (x - 3)^2 + (y - 4)^2. $$ This equation reduces to $$ 6x + 8y = 25, $$ which is the required equation to the locus of P. On geometrical grounds it is clear that the locus is the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the two points O, A and the equation $6x + 8y = 25$ therefore represents this bisector.

Example 8. Find the equation to the locus of a point which is always at distance a from the origin.

If P is the point $(x, y)$ and O is the origin $$ OP^2 = x^2 + y^2. $$ Since $OP = a$, the required equation to the locus of P is $$ x^2 + y^2 = a^2, $$ the equation representing a circle centre the origin and radius a.

Example 9. Find the equation to the locus of a point which moves so that its distance from a line parallel to the axis Oy through the point $(-1,0)$ is equal to its distance from the point $(1,0)$.

In Fig. 103, AB is the line through C$(-1,0)$ parallel to the axis Oy. S is the point $(1, 0)$ and P the point $(x, y)$. PM is the distance of P from the line AB and is clearly equal to the sum of the abscissa of P and the length CO. Hence $$ PM = x + 1. $$ Also, since P and S are respectively the points $(x, y)$ and $(1,0)$, $$ PS^2 = (x - 1)^2 + y^2. $$ Since $PS = PM$, we therefore have $$ (x - 1)^2 + y^2 = (x + 1)^2, $$ or $y^2 = 4x$. This is the required equation to the locus of P. The locus is shown dotted in Fig. 103 and its exact shape could be obtained by plotting the graph of $y^2 = 4x$.

14.8 The intersections of two curves whose equations are known

The coordinates of the points of intersection, or common points, of two curves will satisfy simultaneously the equations of both curves. The problem of finding the coordinates of the points of intersection of two curves therefore reduces to that of finding the solutions of a pair of simultaneous equations.

As an example, consider the points of intersection of the straight line $x - 3y + 7 = 0$ (equation (14.9)) with the curve $$ 3x^2 + 3y^2 - 18x - 20y + 57 = 0. $$ The coordinates of the points of intersection are given by the solutions of the simultaneous equations $$ x - 3y + 7 = 0, $$ $$ 3x^2 + 3y^2 - 18x - 20y + 57 = 0. $$ Substituting the value $x = 3y - 7$, obtained from the first equation, in the second, the ordinates of the common points are given by $$ 3(3y - 7)^2 + 3y^2 - 18(3y - 7) - 20y + 57 = 0. $$ This reduces to $3y^2 - 20y + 33 = 0$, or, $(3y - 11)(y - 3) = 0$. Hence $y = 11/3$ or 3, and from the relation $x = 3y - 7$, the corresponding values of x are 4 and 2. Hence the required coordinates of the points of intersection are $(4, 11/3)$ and $(2, 3)$. If the graphs of the line and curve are plotted and the coordinates of the points of intersection are read from the diagram, they will be found to agree with these values.

Example 10. Find the length of the line joining the points of intersection of the straight line $x - y + 1 = 0$ and the curve $y = 2x^2 - x + 1$.

CHAPTER 15: THE COORDINATE GEOMETRY OF THE STRAIGHT LINE

15.1 An equation of the first degree in x and y represents a straight line

The most general equation of the first degree in x and y is $$ Ax + By + C = 0, \quad (15.1) $$ where A, B and C are constants. Let $P_1(x_1, y_1), P_2(x_2, y_2), P_3(x_3, y_3)$ be any three points lying on the locus represented by the equation (15.1). Since $P_1$ lies on the locus, its coordinates $(x_1, y_1)$ must satisfy equation (15.1) and hence $$ Ax_1 + By_1 + C = 0. $$ Similarly $Ax_2 + By_2 + C = 0$, and $Ax_3 + By_3 + C = 0$. Subtracting the second of these equations from the first and the third from the second we obtain $$ A(x_1 - x_2) + B(y_1 - y_2) = 0 $$ and $A(x_2 - x_3) + B(y_2 - y_3) = 0$. By equating the values of the ratio A/B obtained from these two equations we have $$ \frac{y_1 - y_2}{x_1 - x_2} = -\frac{A}{B} = \frac{y_2 - y_3}{x_2 - x_3} $$ giving $(x_1 - x_2)(y_2 - y_3) = (x_2 - x_3)(y_1 - y_2)$. This can be rearranged as $$ x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2) = 0. $$ From formula (14.6) we see that the area of the triangle formed by the three points $P_1, P_2$ and $P_3$ is zero. Since the points $P_1, P_2, P_3$ are any three points on the locus represented by equation (15.1), the locus must be a straight line; for a curved line could not be such that the area of the triangle formed by joining any three points on it should be zero.

15.2 The equation to a straight line which is parallel to one of the coordinate axes

In Fig. 104, let AB be a line parallel to the axis Oy. Let AB meet the axis Ox in C and let $OC = c$. Let P be any point on the line AB and let its coordinates be $(x, y)$. Since the abscissa of the point P is always c, we have $$ x = c. $$ This relation is true for every point on the line AB and for no other point. The relation $x = c$ is therefore the equation to the line.

Similarly the equation to a line parallel to the axis Ox and at distance d from it is $$ y = d. $$ The coordinate axes are special cases of these lines for which $c = d = 0$. The equation of the axis Ox is therefore $y = 0$ and that of the axis Oy is $x = 0$.

15.3 Special forms of the equation to a straight line

It is often useful to be able to write the general equation $$ Ax + By + C = 0 $$ of a straight line in a form in which the constants A, B, C, or rather the ratios of two of them to the third, are related to some geometrical properties of the line. Such properties might be the slope of the line and the coordinates of a point on it, the intercepts it makes on the coordinate axes and so on. Special forms of the equation to a line in terms of various properties possessed by it are developed in the paragraphs which follow.

15.3 (a) The equation to a line in terms of its slope and its intercept on the y-axis

Let the line have slope m and make an intercept c on the y-axis. Then in Fig. 105 where CP is the line, CR is parallel to the axis Ox and PP' is parallel to the axis Oy, $\tan \theta = m$ and the coordinates of the point C are $(0, c)$. If P be any point on the line CP with coordinates $(x, y)$, the figure shows that $$ CR = OP' = x, $$ $$ PR = PP' - RP' = PP' - OC = y - c, $$ Hence $$ y = mx + c \quad (15.2) $$ is the relation between the coordinates of any point $(x, y)$ on CP and this is the required equation to the line in terms of its slope m and intercept c.

By writing the general equation $Ax + By + C = 0$ in the form $$ y = -\frac{A}{B}x - \frac{C}{B}, $$ and comparing it with (15.2) we see that the ratios A/B, C/B of the constants in the general equation can be expressed in terms of the slope m of the line and its intercept c on the y-axis by $$ -\frac{A}{B} = m, \quad -\frac{C}{B} = c. \quad (15.3) $$ For the line of slope m passing through the origin of coordinates O, $c = 0$ and the equation to such a line is $$ y = mx. $$

15.3 (b) The equation to a line in terms of its slope and the coordinates of a point on it

The equation to any line of slope m is, from (15.2), $$ y = mx + c. $$ If the point whose coordinates are $(x_1, y_1)$ lies on the line, these values of x and y will satisfy the above equation so that $$ y_1 = mx_1 + c. $$ By subtraction we have $$ y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \quad (15.4) $$ as the equation to the line of slope m which passes through the point $(x_1, y_1)$.

Example 1. Find the equation to the straight line which passes through the point $(1,2)$ and makes an angle of $45^\circ$ with the x-axis.

Here $m = \tan 45^\circ = 1, x_1 = 1, y_1 = 2$ and the required equation is $$ y - 2 = (1)(x - 1), $$ or, $x - y + 1 = 0$.

15.3 (c) The equation to a line passing through two given points

If one of the given points has coordinates $(x_1,y_1)$ the equation to a line passing through it is (equation (15.4)) $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. If the second given point has coordinates $(x_2, y_2)$ and the above line passes through it, its equation will be satisfied by $y = y_2, x = x_2$. Hence $$ y_2 - y_1 = m(x_2 - x_1). $$ By division, we have $$ \frac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{y - y_1}{y_2 - y_1} \quad (15.5) $$ as the required equation to the line passing through the two points $(x_1,y_1), (x_2,y_2)$.

Example 2. If B and C are respectively the points $(-1, -3)$ and $(5, -1)$ find the equation to the line BC.

From (15.5) the required equation is $$ \frac{x - (-1)}{5 - (-1)} = \frac{y - (-3)}{-1 - (-3)}, $$ $$ \frac{x + 1}{6} = \frac{y + 3}{2}, $$ giving $x - 3y = 8$.

15.3 (d) The equation to a line in terms of its intercepts on the coordinate axes

Let the line AB (Fig. 106) make intercepts of lengths a and b on the coordinate axes Ox, Oy respectively. Then the line passes through the points $(a, 0)$ and $(0, b)$ and formula (15.5) gives for its equation $$ \frac{x - a}{0 - a} = \frac{y - 0}{b - 0}. $$ This, by cross multiplication, gives $$ bx - ab = -ay, $$ or, after division by ab and a slight rearrangement, $$ \frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} = 1. \quad (15.6) $$ The general equation $Ax + By + C = 0$ can be written $$ \frac{x}{(-C/A)} + \frac{y}{(-C/B)} = 1 $$ and a comparison with equation (15.6) shows that the line $Ax + By + C = 0$ makes intercepts on the coordinate axes of $-C/A$ and $-C/B$.

15.3 (e) The equation to a line in terms of the length of the perpendicular from the origin and the angle this perpendicular makes with the x-axis

In Fig. 107, let the line cut the coordinate axes in A and B, let p be the length of the perpendicular OP drawn from the origin O on to AB and let OP make an angle $\alpha$ with the axis Ox. Then the right-angled triangle OAP gives $$ OA = p \sec \alpha, $$ and, since the angle BOP is $90^\circ - \alpha$, the right-angled triangle BOP gives $$ OB = p \sec (90^\circ - \alpha) = p \csc \alpha. $$ The line AB therefore makes intercepts $p \sec \alpha$ and $p \csc \alpha$ on the coordinate axes and, from (15.6), its equation is $$ \frac{x}{p \sec \alpha} + \frac{y}{p \csc \alpha} = 1, $$ or, $$ x \cos \alpha + y \sin \alpha = p. \quad (15.7) $$ In working problems on the straight line, a correct choice of the form of the equation to the line can often reduce the algebraical work.
